# Contextual Border Design

name: contextual-border-design
description: Use when users mention main content, supplementary content, content borders, macro micro context, content boundaries, main vs supplementary, content scope definition, or article sections.

---

## Purpose

Contextual border design is the discipline of defining the exact boundary between main content and supplementary content on every page in the semantic content network. This boundary determines what the page is fundamentally about (main content) versus what additional context it provides for internal linking and topical reinforcement (supplementary content). Getting this boundary right is critical for search engine interpretation, internal link weight distribution, and the overall coherence of the semantic content network. This skill teaches agents how to draw the line between main and supplementary content, apply macro and micro context correctly, use antonym strategies in supplementary sections, and make index construction decisions.

---

## Core Concepts

### Main Content vs Supplementary Content

Every page has two distinct content zones:

**Main Content = 100% Macro Context**
The main content is the core of the page. It is the complete, self-contained treatment of the page's primary topic. The main content should:
- Address the target query or topic comprehensively
- Contain all the entities, attributes, and contextual vectors required by the content brief
- Be the section that, if it were the only content on the page, would fully satisfy the reader's intent
- Carry the page's primary semantic weight
- Represent the macro context -- the broad, complete picture of what this page is about

**Supplementary Content = Micro Context for Internal Linking**
The supplementary content sits outside the main content boundary. It provides additional context that serves two purposes:
- Contextualizes the main content by connecting it to related topics in the content network
- Creates natural opportunities for internal links to other pages in the semantic content network

Supplementary content is micro context -- small, focused sections that touch on related topics without deeply covering them. Each supplementary section creates a contextual bridge to another page in the network.

### The "Draw a Line" Test

For every page, you should be able to draw a clear, visible line between where the main content ends and where the supplementary content begins. If this line is ambiguous, the page has a contextual border problem.

The test:
1. Read through the page content.
2. Identify the point where the content shifts from directly serving the primary topic to providing tangential or bridging information.
3. Draw the line at that point.
4. Everything above the line is main content. Everything below (or in sidebars, footers, related sections) is supplementary.
5. If you cannot draw a clear line, the page needs restructuring.

In practice, the main content typically comprises the introduction, all primary headings (H2s) that directly address the page's topic, and the conclusion or summary. Supplementary content appears as:
- "Related topics" sections
- "See also" sections
- Brief mentions of adjacent topics with links
- Comparison tables that bridge to other pages
- FAQ sections that address tangentially related questions

### Antonym Strategy in Supplementary Content

One of the most effective strategies for supplementary content is the antonym approach -- including brief coverage of the opposite or contrasting topic to create linking opportunities and contextual contrast.

Example: If the main content page covers "Safest Cities in Europe," the supplementary section might include a brief section referencing "Most Dangerous Cities in Europe" with a link to that page. This creates:
- A natural contextual bridge between two related pages
- Semantic contrast that reinforces the main content's topic (safest is better understood in contrast to most dangerous)
- An internal linking opportunity that feels natural, not forced

The antonym strategy works across many topic types:
- Benefits page --> supplementary mention of drawbacks (linking to drawbacks page)
- Beginner guide --> supplementary mention of advanced techniques (linking to advanced page)
- Pros page --> supplementary mention of cons (linking to cons page)
- Cheapest options --> supplementary mention of premium options (linking to premium page)
- Fastest methods --> supplementary mention of most thorough methods (linking to thorough methods page)

The key rule: the antonym content in the supplementary section must be brief. It exists to create the contextual bridge and internal link, not to deeply cover the contrasting topic. That depth belongs on the linked page.

### Index Construction Decision

One of the most important decisions in contextual border design is determining when a topic deserves its own page versus when it should be included as supplementary content on an existing page. The decision framework:

**Create a new page when:**
- The topic has sufficient search volume to justify its own page
- The topic has enough depth to support a full article (minimum 800+ words of substantive main content)
- The topic represents a distinct entity or entity-attribute combination in the topical map
- Including the topic as supplementary content on an existing page would make that page too long or unfocused
- The topic serves a different audience segment or journey stage than the existing page

**Include as supplementary content when:**
- The topic is a minor subtopic that does not warrant standalone coverage
- The topic primarily exists to provide context or contrast for the main content
- The topic has minimal search volume on its own
- A full article on the topic would be thin or redundant with existing pages
- The topic is better understood in the context of the parent page

When in doubt, lean toward creating a new page. It is easier to merge two thin pages later than to split supplementary content out of a page that has already been indexed and is ranking.

### Link Weight Distribution

The position of a link within the page's content zones affects how much weight it carries:

**Main content links = Heavy weight**
Links placed within the main content body carry the strongest internal linking signal. These links are surrounded by topically relevant content, occur in the section that search engines identify as the page's core content, and pass the most link equity. Use main content links for:
- Links to the most important related pages in the content network
- Links to pages that are contextually essential to understanding the current topic
- Links that represent primary contextual vectors

**Supplementary content links = Lighter weight**
Links placed in supplementary sections (related topics, see also, sidebar content) carry less weight than main content links. The search engine recognizes these as peripheral connections rather than core relationships. Use supplementary links for:
- Links to tangentially related pages
- Antonym/contrast page links
- Links to adjacent topics that provide context but are not central to the page's main topic

**Strategic implication:** The most important internal links in the content network should be placed within main content, not relegated to supplementary sections. If a link is critical to the semantic content network's architecture, it belongs in the main content zone.

### Contextual Border Placement

Practical guidelines for placing the contextual border:

**Before the border (main content):**
- Title and meta description focus
- Introduction establishing the page's primary topic
- All H2 sections that directly address the target query
- Supporting H3 sections within those H2s
- Data, examples, and evidence supporting the main topic
- Conclusion or summary of the main topic

**After the border (supplementary content):**
- "Related Topics" or "See Also" sections
- Brief mentions of contrasting or adjacent topics
- FAQ sections addressing tangential questions
- Comparison bridges to other content network pages
- Navigation aids and category links

**The border itself should be visually and structurally clear.** Use a distinct heading (like "Related Topics" or "Further Reading") to signal the transition. This helps both search engines and readers understand where the main content ends and supplementary context begins.

## Implementation Guidance

When advising on contextual border design:

1. **Audit each page for border clarity.** Can you draw a clear line between main and supplementary content? If not, the page needs restructuring.

2. **Verify main content completeness.** The main content zone must fully cover the page's topic without relying on supplementary content to fill gaps. If important information is in the supplementary zone, it may need to be moved above the border.

3. **Check supplementary content purpose.** Every supplementary section should serve a clear purpose: contextual bridging, internal linking, or antonym contrast. Remove supplementary content that serves no linking or contextual function.

4. **Evaluate link placement.** Map where internal links fall relative to the contextual border. Ensure the most important links are in main content, not supplementary.

5. **Apply the antonym strategy.** For each page, identify whether an antonym or contrast section in the supplementary zone would create valuable internal linking opportunities.

6. **Make index construction decisions.** For topics currently in supplementary sections, evaluate whether they deserve their own page. For thin standalone pages, evaluate whether they should be consolidated as supplementary content on a stronger page.

7. **Document borders in content briefs.** When creating briefs, explicitly specify what belongs in main content and what belongs in supplementary content. Do not leave this decision to the author.

## Advanced Contextual Border Concepts

### Contextual Border as Main/Supplementary Transition
The contextual border provides a slow transition from main content (macro-context) to supplementary content (micro-contexts). It is NOT an abrupt shift but a graduated deepening of the main context that opens connections to adjacent topics. The border section is where the page's macro-context begins to connect with other macro-contexts through related side-topics.

### Grouper Question Strategy
The contextual border uses a grouper question (H2 level = Short Form Question Answering) that deepens the main context while simultaneously connecting to side-topics. H3 headings under this grouper question serve as conditions and qualifiers — connected questions that elaborate on the grouper question from different angles. Word count does NOT determine whether it's long-form or short-form QA; the structural role does.

### Contextual Coverage Balance at the Border
The border must not dilute the prominence of macro-context sections. If supplementary content (after the border) takes up too much of the page, it diminishes the weight of the main content. Calculate the optimal ratio: main content should typically occupy 60-70% of the page, with the border and supplementary content occupying 30-40%.

### Contextual Bridge Activation
From the contextual border, activate both types of bridges: (1) hypertext bridges — internal links to related topical map nodes with strategically selected anchor text, (2) linkless bridges — aligned information that references concepts covered in other pages without explicit links. Both types strengthen the semantic content network's connectivity.

### Border and Passage Indexing
The contextual border zone is particularly important for passage indexing (ranking). Search engines can index and rank individual passages — the border's grouper questions and their answers become discrete rankable units. Design border content to provide standalone, directly responsive answers that work as independent passage ranking targets.

## Key Principles Summary

- Every page has a clear boundary between main content and supplementary content
- Main content represents 100% macro context -- the complete treatment of the page's primary topic
- Supplementary content provides micro context for internal linking and contextual bridging
- The "draw a line" test: if you cannot clearly separate main from supplementary, the page needs restructuring
- Use antonym strategies in supplementary content to create natural linking opportunities (safest vs most dangerous, pros vs cons)
- Create a new page when a topic has sufficient volume, depth, and distinctness; include as supplementary otherwise
- Main content links carry heavy weight; supplementary content links carry lighter weight
- Place the most important internal links within the main content zone
- The contextual border should be visually and structurally clear through distinct headings
