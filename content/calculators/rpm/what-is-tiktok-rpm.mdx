TikTok RPM (Revenue Per Mille) is the amount a creator earns for every 1,000 views, calculated as total earnings divided by total views, multiplied by 1,000. On TikTok's current Creator Rewards Program, RPM typically runs $0.50 to $2.00 per 1,000 qualified views, versus just $0.02 to $0.05 on the legacy Creator Fund. This guide covers the exact formula, how RPM differs from the CPM advertisers pay, what a good RPM looks like in 2026, and the specific levers that move the number.

## RPM Definition and Formula

RPM stands for Revenue Per Mille, where "mille" is Latin for one thousand. In the context of TikTok, RPM represents the money a creator earns for every 1,000 views on their content. It is the single most useful metric for understanding your earning efficiency on the platform because it normalizes revenue against reach, letting you compare performance across videos, time periods, and monetization programs.

The formula is straightforward:

**RPM = (Total Earnings / Total Views) x 1,000**

If you earned $15 from a video that received 10,000 views, your RPM for that video is:

($15 / 10,000) x 1,000 = **$1.50 RPM**

That means you earned $1.50 for every 1,000 views on that piece of content. You can calculate RPM for a single video, a batch of videos over a specific period, or your entire account lifetime. The broader your measurement window, the more reliable the number becomes as a representation of your true earning rate. TikTok does not display RPM directly in analytics, so you calculate it yourself from the earnings and view figures the dashboard reports — the worked example in the FAQ below shows the exact steps.

RPM is a creator-side metric. It reflects what actually lands in your pocket after TikTok takes its share of the advertising revenue. That distinction is what separates RPM from the closely related metric CPM.

## RPM vs CPM -- Key Difference

CPM (Cost Per Mille) and RPM are often confused, but they measure different sides of the same transaction. Understanding the difference prevents you from overestimating your potential earnings.

**CPM is what advertisers pay.** When a brand runs ads on TikTok, they pay a certain amount per 1,000 impressions. This is the CPM -- the cost the advertiser incurs to show their ad to viewers. TikTok's average advertising CPM sits in the $5 to $10 range depending on targeting, placement, and competition, with premium placements climbing above $10.

**RPM is what creators receive.** TikTok keeps a significant portion of the advertising revenue to cover platform operations, content delivery, and profit. What remains is distributed to creators through programs like Creator Rewards. Your RPM is always lower than the CPM advertisers paid because TikTok's revenue share sits between you and the advertiser's full spend.

The gap between CPM and RPM is substantial. If advertisers pay a $10 CPM and your RPM is $1.50, TikTok is retaining roughly 85% of the ad revenue on that inventory. This is the core teaching point that trips up new creators: the CPM figures you see in ad reports are not what you earn.

The practical takeaway: when you read about TikTok's advertising CPM rates, do not confuse those numbers with your income. Divide the reported ad CPM by a factor of 5 to 10 to estimate the corresponding creator RPM, depending on the monetization program and content category. ## What Counts as a Qualified View

Under the Creator Rewards Program, TikTok does not pay on raw view counts. It pays on **qualified views**, and understanding this is essential to calculating an accurate RPM. According to [TikTok's official Creator Rewards documentation](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/creator-rewards-program), a qualified view is a unique view from the For You feed that is watched for longer than 5 seconds. It excludes fraudulent, paid, or promoted views, repeat views from the same account, and "not interested" views. A video must reach 1,000 qualified For You feed views before it starts earning at all.

Payouts also are not a flat views-times-RPM number. TikTok's [FAQ on how rewards are calculated](https://www.tiktok.com/support/faq_detail?id=7543897461245450808) describes a two-part structure: a **Standard Reward** (qualified views multiplied by your RPM) plus an **Additional Reward** that recognizes content craft, engagement, and specialization. Two creators with the same qualified-view count can therefore land on different effective RPMs. When you compute RPM yourself, divide the earnings TikTok reports by your qualified views — using raw views will understate your true rate.

To even enter the program, TikTok requires 10,000+ followers, 100,000+ video views in the last 30 days, an account holder aged 18 or older (19 in South Korea), a Personal Account in good standing, original content, and videos at least 60 seconds long. Millions of views on sub-minute clips can therefore earn $0, which is the most common monetization mistake on the platform.

## Average TikTok RPM in 2026

TikTok RPM varies dramatically depending on which monetization program you are in. The two primary programs produce very different results.

**Creator Rewards Program: $0.50 - $2.00 RPM.** This is TikTok's current flagship monetization program, and it pays 10-40x more per view than its predecessor. The range is wide because RPM depends on niche, audience geography, video length, and engagement quality. Creators who consistently produce longer-form content for US-based audiences in high-value niches routinely see RPM above $1.00. Independent 2026 market data broadly agrees: competitor analysis from [Miraflow](https://miraflow.ai/blog/tiktok-rpm-monetization-2026-how-much-tiktok-pays) reports roughly $0.40 to $1.50 per 1,000 qualified views for US audiences.

**Creator Fund (legacy): $0.02 - $0.05 RPM.** The original Creator Fund pays a fraction of what Creator Rewards offers, averaging about $0.03 per 1,000 views. At the upper end, a $0.05 RPM means $50 per million views. The Fund is legacy — superseded by Creator Rewards in most markets but still active in some regions (the US, UK, Germany, France, Spain, and Italy). If you are eligible for Creator Rewards, switching is one of the highest-impact changes you can make to your earnings.

Here is how TikTok's two programs compare to other platforms per 1,000 views:

| Platform / Program | Typical RPM (per 1,000 views) |
|---|---|
| TikTok Creator Rewards | $0.50 – $2.00 |
| TikTok Creator Fund (legacy) | $0.02 – $0.05 |
| YouTube long-form (AdSense) | $2.00 – $4.00 |
| YouTube Shorts | $0.04 – $0.10 |
| Instagram Reels | $0.01 – $0.03 |
| Twitch | $2.00 – $5.00 |

The gap explains a common complaint: TikTok's legacy Creator Fund pays 50-100x less per view than YouTube long-form. Creator Rewards closes most of that gap, but TikTok short-form still trails YouTube's long-form AdSense rates. ## Factors That Affect Your RPM

Five primary variables determine where your RPM lands within the ranges above. Understanding each one helps you make strategic decisions about your content and audience development.

**Content niche.** This is the largest single driver of RPM variation. Advertisers pay dramatically different rates to reach audiences in different content categories because a finance or software customer is worth far more than a general consumer. The table below shows canonical Creator Rewards RPM ranges by niche:

| Niche | Creator Rewards RPM (per 1,000 qualified views) |
|---|---|
| Finance | $1.50 – $3.00 |
| Tech | $1.00 – $2.50 |
| Education | $0.80 – $2.00 |
| Beauty & Fashion | $0.60 – $1.50 |
| Fitness | $0.60 – $1.50 |
| Food | $0.50 – $1.20 |
| Lifestyle | $0.50 – $1.20 |
| Entertainment | $0.40 – $1.00 |
| Gaming | $0.40 – $1.00 |
| Comedy | $0.30 – $0.80 |
| Other | $0.50 – $1.20 |

Entertainment and comedy content, despite generating the highest view counts, sit at the bottom of the table. For a deeper split of these figures, see our [RPM rates by niche](/data/rpm-rates-by-niche/) data page.

**Audience geography.** Where your viewers are located affects your RPM almost as much as your niche. US views generate roughly 5-50x more revenue per view than views from the lowest-income markets. A creator with 100,000 views from American viewers can outearn a creator with over a million views from predominantly low-CPM markets. Our [TikTok RPM by country](/calculators/rpm/tiktok-rpm-by-country/) breakdown maps the full geographic spread.

**Video length.** The Creator Rewards Program requires videos of at least 60 seconds, and longer videos that maintain high watch time earn more per view. A three-minute video with strong retention generates considerably higher RPM than a barely-qualifying one-minute video, because TikTok can serve more ad impressions against longer content.

**Engagement quality.** TikTok evaluates not just how many people watched but how they interacted. High completion rates, strong like-to-view ratios, meaningful comments, and shares all signal quality content that earns the Additional Reward. Two videos with identical view counts can produce different RPM numbers if one generated significantly stronger engagement signals.

**Seasonality.** Advertiser spending fluctuates throughout the year, and your RPM moves with it. Q4 (October through December) typically produces the highest RPM as brands increase holiday budgets, while January often dips as advertisers reset annual spend. Understanding this cycle — detailed on our [seasonal TikTok RPM](/calculators/rpm/seasonal-tiktok-rpm/) page — helps you avoid panic when your RPM drops after the holidays.

## Common RPM Mistakes

A handful of avoidable errors cause creators to either earn far less than they could or badly miscalculate what they are earning.

**Posting videos under 60 seconds.** This is the most expensive mistake. Sub-minute clips do not qualify for Creator Rewards, so a viral 30-second video can rack up millions of views and pay nothing. If monetization is a goal, structure content to clear the one-minute threshold naturally.

**Using raw views instead of qualified views.** Dividing earnings by total views instead of qualified views produces a misleadingly low RPM. Always use the qualified-view count TikTok reports for Creator Rewards content.

**Confusing CPM with RPM.** Seeing a "$10 TikTok CPM" and assuming you earn $10 per 1,000 views overstates income by roughly 5-10x. That $10 is the advertiser's cost, not your revenue.

**Chasing volume over value.** A million entertainment views to a global audience can earn less than 100,000 finance views to a US audience. Total earnings are RPM multiplied by views, and RPM is often the bigger lever.

**Comparing your RPM to Creator Fund benchmarks.** If your calculator or dashboard benchmarks you against $0.02-$0.05 Fund rates while you are on Creator Rewards, a $1.00 RPM will look "impossibly high" when it is actually normal. Make sure you are comparing against the right program.

## How to Increase Your RPM

Improving your RPM is one of the most efficient ways to grow TikTok income because it raises earnings without requiring more views. To put the levers in dollar terms: 1,000,000 qualified views on the legacy Creator Fund at $0.03 RPM earns about $30, while the same million views on Creator Rewards at $1.00 RPM earns roughly $1,000 — and a US finance creator near $2.00 RPM earns about $2,000. The single view count, three very different outcomes.

The highest-impact moves are clearing the 60-second minimum to access Creator Rewards, shifting your audience toward higher-CPM markets like the US and UK, adding higher-value niche angles (finance, tech, education) to your content, and optimizing for watch time and engagement to earn the Additional Reward. ## FAQ

### What is a good RPM on TikTok in 2026?

On the Creator Rewards Program, anything above $1.00 per 1,000 qualified views is solid, and $1.50-$2.00 is strong — typically achieved by finance, tech, and education creators with US-heavy audiences. Below $0.50 usually signals a low-CPM niche, a non-US audience, weak engagement, or that you are still on the legacy Creator Fund (where $0.04+ counts as excellent).

### How much does TikTok pay per 1,000 views?

Creator Rewards pays about $0.50 to $2.00 per 1,000 qualified views ($500 to $2,000 per million), while the legacy Creator Fund pays roughly $0.02 to $0.05 ($20 to $50 per million). Your exact rate depends on niche, audience country, video length, and engagement. These are estimates from published rate data, not official TikTok figures.

### What is the difference between CPM and RPM on TikTok?

CPM is what advertisers pay per 1,000 impressions (a $5-$10 average on TikTok); RPM is what you receive per 1,000 views after TikTok takes its share. RPM is always lower than CPM — a useful rule of thumb is to divide the ad CPM by 5-10 to estimate the creator RPM.

### Why is my TikTok RPM so low or why did it drop?

The usual causes are a non-US audience, a low-CPM niche (comedy, entertainment, gaming), videos under 60 seconds that do not qualify, weak engagement, or a seasonal Q1 dip after Q4's holiday peak. A sudden drop in January is a market-wide pattern, not a reflection of your content quality.

### Do TikTok videos need to be over 1 minute to make money?

Yes, for the Creator Rewards Program. Videos must be at least 60 seconds long and reach 1,000 qualified For You feed views before earning. Shorter clips can go viral but pay nothing through Creator Rewards, which is why the one-minute minimum is the most common monetization mistake.

### How do I calculate my TikTok RPM if TikTok doesn't show it?

Take the earnings TikTok reports in your Creator dashboard, divide by your qualified view count for the same period, and multiply by 1,000. For example, $45 earned on 50,000 qualified views is ($45 / 50,000) x 1,000 = $0.90 RPM.
