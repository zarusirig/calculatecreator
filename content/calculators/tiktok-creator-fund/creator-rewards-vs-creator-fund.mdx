The TikTok Creator Rewards Program replaced the legacy Creator Fund and pays an estimated $0.50-$2.00 per 1,000 qualified views, versus the Fund's historical $0.02-$0.05 per 1,000 views — a 10-40x difference per view. The Creator Fund is discontinued, so in 2026 there is no real choice between the two: Creator Rewards is TikTok's only in-feed view monetization program, and it requires original videos of at least one minute.

This page compares the two programs line by line — pay model, rates, eligibility, view counting, and payout mechanics — based on [TikTok's official Creator Rewards documentation](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/creator-rewards-program/creator-rewards-program) and published RPM ranges. One caveat before the numbers: TikTok does not publish an official rate card, so every dollar figure here is a planning estimate drawn from TikTok's documentation and aggregated creator reports, not a guarantee.

## Side-by-Side Comparison

TikTok fundamentally restructured how it pays creators for in-feed views. The original Creator Fund launched in 2020 with a fixed pool of money split among participants. Its successor — briefly called the Creativity Program Beta before being renamed the Creator Rewards Program — abandoned the pool model entirely and ties pay to the performance of each individual video.

Here is how the two programs compare across every metric that matters to your earnings:

| Feature | Creator Fund (legacy, discontinued) | Creator Rewards Program (current) |
|---|---|---|
| Status in 2026 | Discontinued — closed to new members | Active |
| Payment model | Fixed pool split among all participants | Performance-based, no fixed pool |
| Estimated rate per 1,000 views | $0.02 - $0.05 (historical) | $0.50 - $2.00 per 1,000 qualified views |
| Estimated pay per 1M views | $20 - $50 | $500 - $2,000 (if all views qualify) |
| Minimum video length | None | 1 minute (60 seconds) |
| Views counted | All video views | Qualified views only |
| Per-video earning threshold | None | 1,000 qualified For You feed views |
| Follower requirement | 10,000+ | 10,000+ |
| Views in last 30 days | 100,000+ | 100,000+ |
| Minimum age | 18+ | 18+ (19 in South Korea) |
| Account type | Personal | Personal only — Business and political/government accounts are not eligible |
| Originality rules | Basic (no reposts) | Strict — original public content; Duets, Stitches, and reposts excluded |
| Earns on older videos | Yes, while enrolled | No — only videos posted after joining earn |

The headline difference is the pay model. The Fund divided a fixed amount of money among everyone; Creator Rewards pays each video based on its own qualified views and quality signals. That single change is why the per-view economics improved by an order of magnitude.

## Pay Rate Differences

The pay gap between these programs is the most consequential change TikTok has made to creator monetization since 2020.

**Old Creator Fund: $0.02 - $0.05 per 1,000 views (historical).** The Creator Fund operated on a fixed-pool model. TikTok committed an initial $200 million for US creators, later announced as $1 billion over three years — but the pool did not grow with the platform. As more creators joined, each individual's share shrank even when their views held steady. A creator generating 10 million views per month often earned only a few hundred dollars, which is exactly why [Creator Fund earnings kept dropping](/guides/why-creator-fund-earnings-dropped/) for so many accounts. Per TikTok's own [Creator Fund support page](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/creator-fund-us/creator-fund), the program is no longer available.

**Creator Rewards: an estimated $0.50 - $2.00 per 1,000 qualified views.** Creator Rewards has no fixed pool. Payouts scale with each video's qualified views and quality signals, so your earnings are not diluted when other creators join. TikTok describes the payout as two parts: a Standard Reward (qualified views multiplied by an RPM) plus an Additional Reward based on content quality, engagement, and topic specialization. Note that the $0.50-$2.00 range is a US-market planning range; independent trackers such as [FlowShorts report $0.50-$1.00+ per 1,000 qualified views](https://flowshorts.app/blog/tiktok-creator-fund) for typical accounts, so treat the top of the range as a ceiling for strong niches, not a default.

### What the same views pay under each program

| Monthly views | Creator Fund (historical, $0.02-$0.05 RPM) | Creator Rewards (est. $0.50-$2.00 RPM) |
|---|---|---|
| 100,000 | $2 - $5 | $50 - $200 |
| 500,000 | $10 - $25 | $250 - $1,000 |
| 1,000,000 | $20 - $50 | $500 - $2,000 |
| 5,000,000 | $100 - $250 | $2,500 - $10,000 |

*The Creator Rewards column assumes every view is a qualified view. In practice, only a portion of raw views qualify, so real payouts land below these figures — see the worked example.*

**Worked example.** Say a one-minute original video pulls 1 million raw views. Under the old Fund at the $0.03 average RPM, that was about $30. Under Creator Rewards, suppose 550,000 of those views are qualified (unique For You feed views watched more than 5 seconds) and your account earns a $1.00 RPM: 550,000 ÷ 1,000 × $1.00 = **$550**, before any Additional Reward. Same video, roughly 18x the payout — and in a high-RPM niche like finance, where site estimates run $1.50-$3.00 per 1,000 qualified views, the same qualified views could reach $825-$1,650.

**Only a share of your raw views count.** Across most accounts, somewhere between 40% and 70% of raw views end up as qualified views — the rest are repeat views from the same account, sub-5-second scroll-bys, off-feed views, or views TikTok flags as paid, promoted, or artificial. That single adjustment is why a video with a million raw views rarely pays the full $500-$2,000: run the math on your qualified-view count, not your headline total. If your qualified share sits near 50%, halve the top-line estimate before you plan around it. The old Fund had no such gate — it paid on every view of every length — which made its rate look simpler even as it paid a fraction as much.

Niche matters as much as geography. Finance, tech, and education content earns toward the top of the range because advertisers pay more to reach those audiences; comedy and entertainment sit near the bottom (roughly $0.30-$1.00 per 1,000 qualified views in our estimates). Under the old Fund, niche barely moved your pay — everyone drew from the same pool at roughly $0.02-$0.05 regardless of topic. Creator Rewards changes that: a finance creator and a comedy creator with identical qualified views can now earn several times apart on the same view count.

## How Creator Rewards Compares to Other Platforms

Zoom out from TikTok and the shift looks even bigger. On a pure per-view basis, Creator Rewards still trails YouTube's long-form ad revenue, but it has closed most of the gap the old Fund left behind. Our house benchmark is that YouTube pays roughly 50-100x more per view than the legacy Creator Fund did — a gap the move to Creator Rewards narrows dramatically.

| Program | Pay model | Est. pay per 1,000 views | Core requirement |
|---|---|---|---|
| TikTok Creator Fund (legacy) | Fixed pool, discontinued | $0.02 - $0.05 | 10K followers, 100K views/30 days |
| TikTok Creator Rewards (current) | Performance-based, no pool | $0.50 - $2.00 (qualified views) | Original videos 1 min+ |
| YouTube long-form (AdSense) | Ad revenue share | $2.00 - $4.00 | YouTube Partner Program threshold |

Two things stand out. First, moving from the Fund to Creator Rewards took TikTok from roughly 50-100x behind YouTube per view to something closer to 2-4x — a different league entirely from the Fund era. Second, per-view rate is only half the equation: TikTok's For You feed puts small accounts in front of large audiences faster than most platforms, so creators often reach the qualified-view volumes that matter on Creator Rewards sooner than they would build comparable watch-page views elsewhere. And view pay is rarely the biggest line on TikTok — LIVE gifts (creators keep roughly 50% of gift value), brand deals, and TikTok Shop's standard 5% affiliate commission frequently out-earn Creator Rewards at small and mid audience sizes. ## Eligibility and Requirements

Both programs share the same baseline entry bar, but Creator Rewards adds strict content-level rules. Everything below matches [TikTok's official eligibility criteria](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/creator-rewards-program/creator-rewards-program).

**Shared baseline requirements:**

- At least 10,000 followers.
- At least 100,000 video views in the last 30 days.
- At least 18 years old (19 in South Korea).
- Account in good standing — no active Community Guidelines violations.
- Located in an eligible country (the US, UK, and much of Europe; check the in-app list for your region).

**Creator Rewards adds four requirements the Fund never had:**

**1. Minimum one-minute video length.** Only original public videos at least 60 seconds long can earn. This is the biggest operational change for creators who built audiences on 15-30 second clips. Shorter videos are not penalized in distribution — they simply do not generate rewards.

**2. Strict originality rules.** TikTok excludes a specific list of formats from earning: Duets and Stitches, reposted or watermarked content, videos with only slight modifications, photo slideshows, loops, text-only videos, and lip-syncs or videos where copyrighted music plays for over a minute. Ads, paid promotion, sponsored content, and Series-linked videos also do not earn rewards.

**3. Personal account required.** Business accounts and political or government accounts are not eligible. If you switched to a Business account for the analytics, you would need to switch back before applying.

**4. Qualified views, not raw views.** A qualified view is a unique video view from the For You feed watched for more than 5 seconds. Fraudulent, paid, promoted, and artificial views are excluded, as are views marked "not interested" and repeat views from the same account (each account counts once per video). Each video must also cross 1,000 qualified For You feed views before it starts generating rewards at all.

The old Creator Fund had none of these content-level rules — any video of any length from an enrolled creator counted. That lower bar is a big part of why its rates were so low: the fixed pool was spread across enormous volumes of short, low-effort content. ## Which Should You Choose

In 2026 the choice has been made for you: the Creator Fund is discontinued, so Creator Rewards is the only option for earning from in-feed views. The real decision is whether adapting your content to Creator Rewards' rules is worth it — and for almost every eligible creator, it is.

**If your content is already 1+ minute and original,** joining is a pure upgrade. There is no scenario where staying unmonetized beats an estimated $0.50-$2.00 per 1,000 qualified views on videos you were going to post anyway.

**If your format is built on sub-60-second clips,** you have a strategy question. Extending a 20-second comedy bit to a minute with filler will tank watch time and hurt both distribution and your RPM. The better path is a split strategy: keep short clips as audience-building and algorithm fuel, and develop one or two formats that naturally run 1-3 minutes — storytimes, tutorials, breakdowns, behind-the-scenes — as your monetized layer. A single one-minute video earning Creator Rewards rates can out-earn dozens of short clips under the old Fund's math.

**If you don't qualify yet** (under 10,000 followers or 100,000 views in the last 30 days), focus on growth first and treat monetization as the milestone, not the starting point. Our [income distribution data](/data/earnings/tiktok-creator-income-distribution/) shows how earnings scale across follower tiers, and view-based payouts are only one stream — LIVE gifts, brand deals, and TikTok Shop often pay more at small and mid audience sizes.

## Common Mistakes When Comparing the Two Programs

A few misreads cost creators real money when they model the switch:

- **Treating raw views as qualified views.** Only unique For You feed views watched past 5 seconds count under Creator Rewards, and each video needs 1,000 qualified views before it earns anything. If you plug raw view counts into a $0.50-$2.00 RPM, you will overestimate — assume a share of your views drops out first.
- **Assuming the Fund's rates still apply.** The Creator Fund is discontinued. Any calculator or article quoting $0.02-$0.05 as your *current* pay is describing history, not what you earn today.
- **Expecting old videos to earn.** Creator Rewards is not retroactive. Your back catalogue earns nothing under the program; only videos posted after you join generate rewards.
- **Padding short videos to reach 60 seconds.** Stretching a 20-second idea to a minute with filler destroys watch time, which suppresses distribution *and* lowers your RPM. Only lengthen content that genuinely supports it.
- **Reading the $2.00 ceiling as a default.** The top of the range is for strong niches like finance in high-CPM markets. Model a floor and a ceiling, and treat the $0.50-$1.00 band as a realistic middle for most accounts.

## How to Switch Programs

There is no in-app "switch" anymore — TikTok wound the Creator Fund down and moved view monetization to Creator Rewards, so former Fund members and new creators alike go through the same application. Here is the current process:

**Step 1: Check your numbers.** Confirm you have 10,000+ followers, 100,000+ views in the last 30 days, a Personal account in good standing, and that you are 18 or older (19 in South Korea). If you are short on either growth metric, wait until you clear it with some margin — the 30-day view window is recalculated on a rolling basis.

**Step 2: Apply through TikTok Studio.** In the app, go to Profile, tap the Menu button, open TikTok Studio, and select Creator Rewards Program. Submit the application; TikTok says it typically replies within about 3 days.

**Step 3: If rejected, appeal or reapply.** You can appeal within 30 days of a rejection. If the appeal window passes, you can submit a new application 30 days after it closes. Most rejections trace back to a metric dipping below threshold during processing or an unresolved account standing issue.

**Step 4: Post new content — the program is not retroactive.** Only videos uploaded after you join can collect rewards. Your back catalog will not start earning, so time your application before a content push, not after one.

**Step 5: Rebuild your format around qualified views.** Aim for original videos of 1-3 minutes with a strong hook in the first 5 seconds — remember, a view only qualifies once someone watches past 5 seconds on the For You feed, and each video needs 1,000 qualified views before it earns anything. Review your analytics weekly and compare RPMs across formats; the [per-view rate breakdown](/calculators/tiktok-creator-fund/creator-fund-pay-per-view/) shows which factors move the number most.

## FAQ

### Is the TikTok Creator Fund still available in 2026?

No. TikTok discontinued the Creator Fund, and its [official support page](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/creator-fund-us/creator-fund) confirms it is no longer available. The Creator Rewards Program is the current view-based monetization program, and all Fund-era rates ($0.02-$0.05 per 1,000 views) are historical reference points only.

### How much more does Creator Rewards pay than the Creator Fund?

Roughly 10-40x more per view, based on published planning ranges. The legacy Fund paid about $20-$50 per million views; Creator Rewards pays an estimated $500-$2,000 per million qualified views, with the exact figure depending on niche, audience geography, and watch time. Because only a portion of raw views count as qualified views, real payouts usually land below the top of that range.

### Do videos have to be longer than one minute to earn?

Yes. Creator Rewards only pays on original public videos that are at least one minute long. Shorter videos still get distributed normally — they just do not generate rewards.

### Will my old videos earn money after I join Creator Rewards?

No. The program is not retroactive. Only videos uploaded after you join can collect rewards, which is why it makes sense to apply before a planned content push rather than after one.

### How much does TikTok pay for 1 million views?

Historically the Creator Fund paid about $20-$50 per million views. The current Creator Rewards Program pays an estimated $500-$2,000 per million *qualified* views, depending on niche, audience geography, and watch time. Because only unique For You feed views watched past 5 seconds count, real payouts on a million *raw* views usually land below the top of that range.

### Can Business accounts join Creator Rewards?

No. TikTok requires a Personal account; Business accounts and political or government accounts are explicitly ineligible. If you run a Business account, you would need to switch account types before applying.

### What replaced the TikTok Creator Fund?

The Creator Rewards Program replaced it. TikTok wound down the fixed-pool Creator Fund and moved in-feed view monetization to Creator Rewards — a performance-based program (briefly tested as the Creativity Program Beta) that pays an estimated $0.50-$2.00 per 1,000 qualified views on original videos at least one minute long. There is no separate "new fund" to join; Creator Rewards is the single successor program for earning from For You feed views.

### Is Creator Rewards worth it compared to LIVE gifts or brand deals?

It depends on your audience size and format. Creator Rewards rewards view volume, so it pays best for accounts that reliably clear large qualified-view counts on one-minute-plus original videos. At small and mid audience sizes, LIVE gifts (creators keep roughly 50% of gift value) and brand deals often out-earn view-based pay per hour of effort. Most established creators run Creator Rewards alongside those streams rather than choosing one.
