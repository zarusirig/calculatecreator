A good TikTok engagement rate in 2026 is **5% to 10%** using the view-based formula, and anything above **10%** is excellent. The platform-wide average is **4.07%**, so clearing 5% already puts you ahead of most accounts. What counts as "good" shifts with your follower count and niche — a 5% rate is merely average for a 3,000-follower comedy account but exceptional for a 500,000-follower creator.

Here is the quick-reference scale the influencer industry uses. TikTok publishes no official engagement benchmarks, so these are conventions, not platform rules:

| Engagement rate | Rating |
| --- | --- |
| 10% or higher | Excellent |
| 5% – 10% | Good |
| 2% – 5% | Average |
| Below 2% | Below average |

Every threshold above uses the canonical view-based formula — likes, comments, shares, and saves divided by views. The rest of this page shows where "good" lands once you factor in follower tier, niche, and what brands actually pay for.

## Average TikTok Engagement Rates in 2026

The average TikTok engagement rate in 2026 is **4.07%** across all follower tiers and niches, measured on a view basis with saves included. That is down from 4.18% in 2025 — a year-over-year dip of 0.11 percentage points, driven mostly by heavier competition among the smallest accounts.

Even at 4.07%, TikTok leads every major platform on engagement. For comparable accounts, Instagram Reels average about **1.48%** and YouTube Shorts around **2.1%**. TikTok's discovery-first architecture explains the gap: the For You Page surfaces videos on interest signals rather than follow relationships, so a strong video from a 500-follower account reaches the same audience as one from a 5-million-follower account. That structure generates more interactions per view than platforms where reach is locked behind the follow graph.

Methodology changes the answer dramatically, so check it before you compare your number to anything online. Our 4.07% is view-based. Independent trackers that also divide by views land nearby — IQFluence, citing Socialinsider, reports a [4.20% median TikTok engagement rate by views for 2026](https://iqfluence.io/public/blog/social-media-benchmarks). Sources that divide by *followers* instead report much higher figures, some placing TikTok above 7%, because most videos reach far more viewers than the creator has followers. Neither is wrong; they answer different questions. This page uses the view-based method, which is what TikTok Analytics and most brand tools default to. [Hootsuite's engagement-rate guide](https://blog.hootsuite.com/calculate-engagement-rate/) walks through all six denominator variants if you want to see how much the choice matters.

## Engagement Rate Benchmarks by Follower Count

Follower count is the single biggest factor in what a good engagement rate looks like. Smaller accounts almost always post higher percentages because their audiences are tighter and more invested. As accounts grow, the percentage falls even as total interactions climb.

The YoY column splits by tier: the smallest tiers lost ground in 2026 while mid-tier and larger accounts gained. The pattern tracks a broader shift toward quality signals — larger, more consistent accounts held their audiences while nano and micro creators faced more competition for the same attention. Third-party benchmarks point the same direction; [Nowadays Media's 2026 tier study](https://nowadays.media/blog/influencer-engagement-rate-benchmarks-2026-by-platform-niche-follower-tier/) reports a comparable Nano-to-Mega decline, though its follower-based method produces higher absolute numbers and treats micro as 10K-100K.

### Nano-Influencers (1K-10K Followers)

Nano-influencers see engagement rates between **8% and 15%**, with a 2026 tier average of **9.32%**. Accounts here regularly exceed 15% when their content targets a tight community or specific interest group.

This happens because nano audiences are built on genuine connection. Followers at this stage tend to be real fans who found the creator through niche content, not casual scrollers who followed after a single viral hit. Comment sections are more conversational, and viewers share content more readily with friends who share the same interest.

If you sit below 8% in this tier, examine your content strategy. You may be pulling views from outside your core audience, which inflates the denominator without matching engagement, or your content may not prompt enough interaction. A good rate at the nano level is 10% or above, which signals strong audience connection and positions you for early brand partnerships.

### Micro-Influencers (10K-50K Followers)

Micro-influencers see engagement rates between **5% and 8%**, with a 2026 average of **5.96%**. This tier is where the natural decline becomes noticeable — as your audience grows, a larger portion of your followers become passive viewers, and crossing 10K followers pushes your content into broader For You Page testing where new viewers interact at lower rates.

A micro-influencer holding 7% or higher is performing well above tier average and is highly attractive to brands. Even the 5% floor clears the "good" threshold and is more than sufficient for brand-deal negotiations.

### Mid-Tier Creators (50K-200K Followers)

Mid-tier creators land between **3% and 5%** engagement, averaging **3.81%** in 2026. At this level your content reaches a broad, diverse audience, and the intimate engagement of smaller accounts becomes structurally hard to maintain.

Watch the trend more than the absolute number. A mid-tier account holding 4% steadily for months is stronger than one that spiked to 8% and is now sliding. Creators who sustain 4.5% to 5%+ here sit in the top quartile for their follower count — the tier where brands concentrate significant partnership budgets, and where a strong rate translates directly into higher deal values.

### Macro and Mega Influencers (200K+)

Macro-influencers (200K-1M followers) average **2.64%** and typically range **2% to 4%**. Mega-influencers (1M+) average **1.87%** and range **1% to 3%**.

These numbers look small next to nano accounts, but the math changes at scale. A mega creator with 2 million followers at 2% still generates roughly 40,000 interactions per post. A nano creator at 12% on 5,000 followers generates 600. Total impact matters as much as the percentage.

At this level "good" shifts. A macro creator holding 3% or higher is performing exceptionally. If a mega account slides below 1%, that is the signal to investigate content quality or posting cadence. Notably, macro and mega were the only tiers to gain engagement year over year in 2026.

## Why Engagement Rate Matters for Monetization

Engagement rate is more than a vanity metric. It directly determines how much you earn from brand partnerships and how much organic reach the algorithm gives your content.

### Brand Deal Thresholds and Dollar Impact

Most brands and agencies set a minimum engagement rate when selecting creators for paid partnerships. In 2026 the working minimum is **4%** — below it, many brands pass regardless of follower count. Premium brands look for **6%+**, and creators in the high-engagement band of roughly 5% to 10%+ can command a **20% to 50% premium** on their rates.

Engagement does not just win the deal; it multiplies the fee. ### Algorithm and Distribution Impact

Engagement also shapes how far your content travels. TikTok has not published its ranking weights, but creators and analysts consistently observe that strong early engagement — likes, comments, shares, saves, and watch-through in the first 30 to 60 minutes — precedes wider distribution. When a video earns those signals quickly, it tends to get served to progressively larger audiences.

That creates a compounding effect: strong early engagement drives broader distribution, which drives more views, which drives more engagement. Completion rate appears to be the heaviest of these signals. Content that holds viewers to the end travels furthest — industry benchmarks put "good" completion at 60% to 80% and excellent at 80%+, though shorter videos naturally complete at higher rates. The practical takeaway: your engagement rate is not just a report card for brands, it is the ignition for reach. ## How to Calculate Your TikTok Engagement Rate

The site-standard, view-based formula is:

**Engagement Rate = (Likes + Comments + Shares + Saves) / Views x 100**

Saves belong in the numerator because they are one of the strongest quality signals a viewer can send, and TikTok Analytics reports them alongside likes, comments, and shares. Worked example — a video with 5,000 likes, 300 comments, 200 shares, and 250 saves on 100,000 views:

(5,000 + 300 + 200 + 250) / 100,000 x 100 = **5.75%**, a solidly "good" result.

Some agencies still use a follower-based formula, dividing by followers instead of views. It runs higher because most TikToks reach well beyond a creator's follower count, so label which method a benchmark uses before comparing. The view-based method fits TikTok best because algorithmic distribution decouples views from followers — a 10K-follower account can pull 2 million views on one video.

A quick component check: your likes-to-views ratio on its own should track your tier's like rate — roughly **6.84%** for nano, **4.21%** for micro, **2.63%** for mid-tier, **1.78%** for macro, and **1.24%** for mega in 2026, so a typical micro creator earns about 4 to 5 likes per 100 views. Likes make up 66% to 73% of total engagement, which means a healthy like rate is necessary but not sufficient. If your like rate matches your tier but your overall number still lags, the gap is in saves, shares, and comments — the higher-signal interactions that move you from "average" to "good."

For your overall rate rather than a single video's rate, average across your last 20 to 30 posts to smooth out viral hits and duds. Pull the raw numbers from TikTok Analytics ([official guide to your analytics](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/creator-and-business-accounts/understanding-your-analytics)) or run them through our [TikTok engagement rate calculator guide](/guides/tiktok-engagement-rate-calculator-guide/) to get your rate instantly.

## What Actually Moves Your Engagement Rate

If you are below the benchmark for your tier and niche, a handful of levers move the number more than anything else:

- **The first-second hook.** Viewers who scroll past before your content registers never get the chance to like, comment, or save, so watch-through drives everything downstream.
- **Save- and share-worthy content.** Likes are easy but low-signal. Content that teaches something (save-worthy) or that a viewer wants to send to a specific person (share-worthy) lifts the whole rate.
- **Direct, specific asks.** A pointed question such as "which of these would you try first?" outperforms a generic "let me know what you think."
- **Fast comment replies.** Responding in the first hour doubles your comment count, since your replies count, and keeps the section active.
- **Consistency.** Posting four to five times a week or more trains both the algorithm and your audience.

## FAQ

### What is a good engagement rate on TikTok in 2026?

A good TikTok engagement rate in 2026 is 5% to 10% by the view-based formula, and above 10% is excellent. Because the platform average is 4.07%, any rate above 5% already beats most accounts, but adjust for your tier: 10%+ is the "good" bar for nano accounts, while 3%+ is strong for macro creators.

### What is the average TikTok engagement rate in 2026?

The average TikTok engagement rate is 4.07% across all tiers and niches, measured view-based with saves included, down from 4.18% in 2025. That still leads Instagram Reels at about 1.48% and YouTube Shorts at around 2.1% for comparable accounts.

### Do saves count toward TikTok engagement rate?

Yes, saves count toward the canonical engagement rate. The formula counts likes, comments, shares, and saves in the numerator, divided by views. Saves are one of the strongest signals a viewer can send, and TikTok Analytics reports them next to your other metrics, so leaving them out understates your true rate.

### What engagement rate do brands look for on TikTok?

Most brands set a working minimum around 4%, premium brands look for 6%+, and creators in the 5% to 10%+ range can command a 20% to 50% premium on their rates. Below 4%, many brands pass regardless of follower count.

### Is a 10% engagement rate good on TikTok?

A 10% engagement rate is excellent by industry convention and outstanding for any account above the nano tier. For a nano creator with 1K to 10K followers it sits right around the "good" line, since nano accounts average 9.32% and often exceed 15%.

### Do micro-influencers really have higher engagement than macro creators?

Yes, micro-influencers average 5.96% versus 2.64% for macro accounts, roughly double. Smaller audiences are tighter and more invested, and the algorithm tests small accounts with more tightly-matched viewers, so a larger share of the audience interacts.
