TikTok engagement rate falls as follower count rises — from about **9.32%** for nano accounts (1K-10K followers) down to **1.87%** for mega accounts (1M+), against a platform-wide average of **4.07%** in 2026. That inverse pattern is the most reliable benchmark in creator analytics, and it means "good" is defined entirely by your tier: a 5% rate is below par for a nano creator but exceptional for a macro one.

All figures on this page use the canonical view-based formula — likes, comments, shares, and saves divided by views. That matters, because follower-based benchmarks report far higher numbers for the same accounts.

## Engagement Rate Benchmarks by Tier (2026)

TikTok engagement rates follow a consistent inverse pattern: the smaller the account, the higher the percentage. This is neither a flaw in large accounts nor an achievement for small ones — it is a structural outcome of how audiences form and how the algorithm distributes content at different scales.

The benchmarks below use the site's canonical tier boundaries and the 2026 tier averages from our [average engagement rate by follower count study](/data/engagement/avg-engagement-rate-follower-count-2026/). Your individual rate varies by niche, content quality, and posting consistency, but these ranges give you a reliable baseline.

| Follower tier | Follower range | 2026 average ER | Typical range | YoY change | Brand deal base/post |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Nano | 1K – 10K | 9.32% | 8% – 15% | -0.41 pp | $50 – $200 |
| Micro | 10K – 50K | 5.96% | 5% – 8% | -0.28 pp | $200 – $1,000 |
| Mid-tier | 50K – 200K | 3.81% | 3% – 5% | +0.12 pp | $1,000 – $5,000 |
| Macro | 200K – 1M | 2.64% | 2% – 4% | +0.09 pp | $5,000 – $15,000 |
| Mega | 1M+ | 1.87% | 1% – 3% | +0.15 pp | $15,000 – $50,000 |
| **All accounts** | **—** | **4.07%** | **—** | **-0.11 pp** | **—** |

These tier boundaries are the site standard, and they matter when you compare charts. Some benchmark providers define micro as 10K-100K and mid-tier as 100K-500K, which shifts every average — a creator we classify as mid-tier (50K-200K) would sit in another source's micro band and appear to "underperform." Before comparing your rate to any external table, confirm two things: the denominator (views versus followers) and the tier cutoffs.

**Nano-influencers (1K-10K followers) average 9.32%.** Nano accounts produce the highest engagement percentages on TikTok, and accounts in this tier with strong niche focus regularly hit 12% to 15%. Even generalist creators here typically clear 8%. If you have fewer than 10,000 followers and your rate is below 8%, something in your content strategy needs attention — you are underperforming the baseline for your tier.

**Micro-influencers (10K-50K followers) average 5.96%.** The micro tier is where the engagement decline becomes noticeable. Crossing 10K followers introduces your content to broader algorithmic distribution, which brings in viewers who are less invested in your specific content. A micro-influencer maintaining 7%+ is performing exceptionally, and the 5% floor of this range still represents strong performance that is more than sufficient for brand partnerships.

**Mid-tier creators (50K-200K followers) average 3.81%.** Mid-tier accounts face audience diversification. At this scale, your follower base mixes loyal fans, casual followers who engaged with one viral video, and viewers who follow broadly within your niche. Holding above 4% here indicates consistently strong content, and creators who sustain 4.5% to 5% land in the top quartile at their follower count.

**Macro-influencers (200K-1M followers) average 2.64%.** Macro accounts feel the full weight of audience dilution. A large portion of followers at this level are passive — they followed once and consume content intermittently. The percentage drops, but total interactions per post rise sharply. A macro influencer averaging 3%+ outperforms the vast majority of accounts at this scale.

**Mega-influencers (1M+ followers) average 1.87%.** Mega accounts operate by different rules. The percentage looks low next to nano or micro tiers, but the absolute numbers are massive. A 2% engagement rate on an account with 5 million followers generates 100,000 interactions per post. At this tier, maintaining above 2% signals a healthy, engaged audience, while rates below 1% suggest audience fatigue or a large share of inactive followers.

For a deeper side-by-side, see our [micro vs macro engagement rate](/data/engagement/micro-vs-macro-engagement-rate/) comparison.

**The percentage hides the absolute number.** A lower rate does not mean less impact, because larger accounts pull far more views per video. Converting each tier's average rate into engagements per 1,000 views shows what the percentage actually delivers:

| Tier | Avg ER | Engagements per 1,000 views |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nano | 9.32% | ~93 |
| Micro | 5.96% | ~60 |
| Mid-tier | 3.81% | ~38 |
| Macro | 2.64% | ~26 |
| Mega | 1.87% | ~19 |

A nano creator wins on interaction per 1,000 views, but a mega account plays a different game — its videos routinely reach millions of views, so a 1.87% rate still produces tens of thousands of engagements per post. Across platforms, TikTok's tier averages still lead: even mega accounts at 1.87% clear Instagram Reels' roughly 1.48% platform average, and TikTok's overall 4.07% nearly doubles YouTube Shorts at around 2.1% for comparable accounts.

## What Makes Up the Rate: Component Benchmarks

Your engagement rate is the sum of four separate metrics, and each one has its own benchmark by tier. Breaking the rate into components tells you *which* interaction is dragging your number down. The table below shows the 2026 average for each component as a percentage of views:

| Tier | Like rate | Comment rate | Share rate | Save rate |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Nano | 6.84% | 0.91% | 0.72% | 0.85% |
| Micro | 4.21% | 0.58% | 0.51% | 0.66% |
| Mid-tier | 2.63% | 0.37% | 0.38% | 0.43% |
| Macro | 1.78% | 0.24% | 0.31% | 0.31% |
| Mega | 1.24% | 0.16% | 0.25% | 0.22% |

Likes dominate the total at every tier, making up roughly 66% to 73% of all engagements. That is why a video can feel like it is "getting likes" and still post a mediocre rate — likes are the easiest, lowest-signal interaction. The other three components separate strong accounts from average ones.

**Save rate is the component most worth optimizing.** Saves signal that a viewer found the content useful enough to return to, and industry benchmarks rate a save rate (saves divided by views) of 5%+ as excellent, 2% to 5% as good, 0.5% to 2% as average, and below 0.5% as below average. A micro account at the tier-average 0.66% save rate sits in the average band, so pushing save-worthy content is the fastest structural lever for that tier.

To diagnose your own account, compare each component against your tier row. If your like rate matches the benchmark but your save and share rates trail, your content is entertaining but not useful or share-worthy — a fixable content problem rather than an audience problem.

## Calculate Your Rate for Your Tier

Knowing your tier average is only useful once you measure your own rate against it. The canonical formula is view-based with saves included:

**Engagement Rate = (Likes + Comments + Shares + Saves) / Views x 100**

Pull the four interaction counts and the view count for each of your last 20 to 30 videos from TikTok Analytics ([official analytics guide](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/creator-and-business-accounts/understanding-your-analytics)), then average them so a single viral hit or flop does not distort the picture. Here is a worked example built from the exact 2026 micro-tier component rates. A micro account (10K-50K followers) posts a video that reaches 100,000 views with 4,210 likes, 580 comments, 510 shares, and 660 saves:

(4,210 + 580 + 510 + 660) / 100,000 x 100 = **5.96%**

That lands precisely on the micro tier average — right at benchmark, clearing the 4% brand minimum, with headroom to reach the top of the tier by lifting the two weakest components. The same 100,000 views tell a different story at other tiers. A nano account interacting at its tier rates would post roughly 9,320 total engagements for a 9.32% rate on identical views, while a macro account would post about 2,640 for 2.64%. The interaction counts shrink as the tier climbs, but the method never changes: the denominator is always views, never followers. Divide by followers instead and the same micro video reads far higher, which is why you should always confirm the denominator before comparing your number to any external chart.

## Why Smaller Accounts Get Higher Engagement

The inverse relationship between follower count and engagement rate is one of the most consistent patterns in social media, and it is especially pronounced on TikTok. Three factors drive it.

**Personal connection scales poorly.** A creator with 3,000 followers can realistically interact with a meaningful share of their audience. They reply to comments, recognize repeat viewers, and make content that feels directed at a specific community. That personal touch drives reciprocal engagement — viewers who feel seen interact more. At 500,000 followers, that level of individual interaction becomes physically impossible, and engagement reflects the shift.

**Algorithmic distribution broadens the audience.** TikTok's For You Page pushes content to users based on interest signals. When an account is small, the algorithm tests content with a relatively targeted audience whose behavior closely matches the creator's niche. As the account grows and videos reach wider audiences, the algorithm serves content to viewers with weaker interest alignment. These broader audiences watch and scroll, but they interact at lower rates than the core niche audience.

**Follower quality degrades with growth.** Early followers tend to be genuine fans who discovered the creator through niche content and made a deliberate choice to follow. Later followers increasingly include people who followed after a single viral video, trend-chasers, and passive accounts. This dilution of follower quality accounts for a significant portion of the engagement decline at higher follower counts.

Understanding these mechanics reframes how you read your own numbers. A declining engagement percentage as you grow is normal and expected. The relevant question is whether your rate is declining faster or slower than the benchmark for your tier.

## What Brands Look For at Each Tier

Engagement benchmarks matter most in brand partnerships, where they directly affect whether you get deals and how much you get paid. Brands evaluate creators differently by tier, and the working minimum engagement rate brands look for in 2026 is **4%** — premium brands want **6%+**.

**For nano and micro-influencers, engagement rate is the primary metric.** Brands working with smaller creators are buying engagement, not reach, and they expect rates at the higher end of the tier benchmark — 10%+ for nano, 7%+ for micro. A nano-influencer with a 12% rate is a far more attractive partner than one with 6%, even at identical follower counts. At this level, your engagement rate is your resume.

**For mid-tier creators (50K-200K), consistency matters as much as the number.** Brands evaluating this tier pull data from your last 30 to 90 days and look for stability. A creator who averages 4.5% consistently is more appealing than one who swings between 2% and 8%, because predictable performance reduces campaign risk.

**For macro and mega influencers (200K+), total reach becomes the primary value.** At this scale brands accept lower percentage rates because they are buying distribution — the ability to put a message in front of hundreds of thousands or millions of people. A mega influencer with a 1.8% rate and 3 million followers generates 54,000 interactions per post, exceeding the total reach of most nano-influencers entirely. The calculus shifts from efficiency to scale.

Engagement also multiplies the fee, not just the odds of landing a deal. Take a micro-creator with 30,000 followers at the tier-average 5.96%. That rate clears the 4% brand minimum and sits in the "good" band. Using the mid-point micro base rate of about $600 and our brand-deal engagement multipliers (modeling assumptions, not TikTok payouts):

| Creator | Engagement | Multiplier | Estimated fee |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |

| Micro, 30K followers | under 2% (below avg) | 0.7x | ~$420 |

The micro-influencer sweet spot is worth highlighting. Brands in 2026 keep shifting budget toward micro-creators (10K-50K) precisely because this tier balances high engagement with meaningful reach. Micro accounts deliver roughly double the rate of macro accounts while still reaching audiences large enough to move business results. Both use different denominators than our view-based method, so their absolute figures run higher.

## Using These Benchmarks

These benchmarks serve three practical purposes for your creator strategy.

**Diagnose your current performance.** Compare your average rate against the range for your tier. Within range means normal performance. Above range is a competitive advantage to leverage in brand negotiations. Below range signals a need to adjust content, posting schedule, or audience targeting — and the component table above tells you which interaction to fix first.

**Set realistic growth expectations.** A nano-influencer with a 13% rate should expect that number to fall while growing. Planning for it prevents the demoralization that hits creators when they cross into a new tier and watch the percentage drop. Absolute interactions rising while the percentage declines is a sign of healthy growth, not decline.

**Benchmark against competitors.** Knowing your tier average lets you evaluate other creators in your niche. If a competitor at a similar follower count posts a higher rate, study their content. If your rate is higher, use it as a data point to justify premium pricing in brand deals.

## FAQ

### What is a good TikTok engagement rate for my follower count?

A good rate depends on your tier: 10%+ for nano (1K-10K), 7%+ for micro (10K-50K), 4%+ for mid-tier (50K-200K), and 3%+ for macro (200K-1M). The 2026 tier averages are 9.32%, 5.96%, 3.81%, and 2.64% respectively, against a 4.07% platform average.

### Why do micro-influencers have higher engagement than macro influencers?

Micro-influencers average 5.96% versus 2.64% for macro accounts because smaller audiences are tighter and more invested, and the algorithm tests small accounts with more tightly-matched viewers. As accounts grow, follower quality dilutes and distribution broadens to less-aligned audiences, so the percentage falls even as total interactions rise.

### What engagement rate do brands expect at each follower tier?

Brands set a working minimum of 4% and want 6%+ for premium deals, but they weight it differently by tier: engagement is the primary metric for nano and micro creators, consistency matters most for mid-tier, and total reach outweighs percentage for macro and mega accounts.

### How is TikTok engagement rate calculated — by views or followers?

The canonical formula divides likes, comments, shares, and saves by views, then multiplies by 100. Follower-based formulas divide by followers instead and report higher numbers, because most TikToks reach far more viewers than the creator has followers, so always label which method a benchmark uses.

### What is the average engagement rate for a 100K TikTok account?

A 100,000-follower account falls in the mid-tier band (50K-200K), which averages 3.81% in 2026 with a typical range of 3% to 5%. Holding above 4% at this size indicates consistently strong content and places you in the upper half of your tier.
