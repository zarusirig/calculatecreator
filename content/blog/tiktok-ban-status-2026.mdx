## Is TikTok Banned in the US Right Now?

No. TikTok continues to operate in the United States, the app is downloadable, Creator Rewards and TikTok Shop payouts are processing, and advertisers are still spending. A hard shutdown is not in effect and has not appeared imminent through the first half of 2026.

What remains in place is the legal framework that *could* force a ban or a forced sale. The Protecting Americans from Foreign Adversary Controlled Applications Act (PAFACA) is valid law, its constitutionality is settled, and ByteDance has not completed a full divestiture of TikTok's US operations. Instead of a clean resolution, the situation has settled into a pattern of extended deadlines and negotiation. TikTok itself acknowledges the ongoing rule changes and uncertainty on its own [2026 rule-changes page](https://www.tiktok.com/en/trending/detail/tiktok-announces-2026-rule-changes).

For creators, that means the honest answer to "is TikTok getting banned" is *not right now, and not obviously soon — but it is not off the table either.* The rest of this page lays out how we got here, what is legally true in 2026, what a ban would actually do to your earnings, and the concrete steps that protect you regardless of the outcome. ## The Legal Landscape in 2026

The Supreme Court's 9–0 ruling in January 2025 settled the core legal question: the divest-or-ban law is constitutional. The Court held that the statute targets foreign ownership and control, not protected speech, so it survives First Amendment scrutiny. That means no one is realistically going to win a lawsuit that makes the law disappear. What is left is political and commercial.

Three moving parts still shape the outcome:

- **CFIUS review.** The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States has to sign off on any restructuring. Separating TikTok's US algorithm and data infrastructure from ByteDance's global systems is technically hard, and that complexity is a big part of why deadlines keep slipping.
- **A US data-and-algorithm arrangement.** Frameworks under discussion involve US-based control of American user data and the recommendation system — the lineage of the earlier "Project Texas" work with Oracle. Reports of an Oracle audit of the US recommendation engine in late 2025 point in this direction.
- **Congressional pressure.** Some lawmakers have pushed to bar further extensions and force enforcement. None of those efforts had forced a shutdown as of mid-2026, but they keep the timeline politically live.

Alongside the ban question, TikTok has rolled out a wave of 2026 policy and compliance changes covering branded and AI-content disclosure, LIVE eligibility, and monetization rules. Darkroom Agency's [2026 TikTok policy guide](https://www.darkroomagency.com/observatory/what-brands-need-to-know-about-tiktok-new-rules-2026) is a useful brand-and-creator reference for those rules, which matter for your earnings whether or not a divestiture ever closes.

## What a Ban Would Actually Mean for Your Earnings

The reason the ban question matters is money, so it helps to attach real numbers to it. TikTok's Creator Rewards Program pays an estimated **$0.50 to $2.00 per 1,000 qualified views** — roughly **$500 to $2,000 per million qualified views** — for original videos that are 60 seconds or longer and surface in the For You feed. RPMs vary sharply by niche: finance content runs highest at an estimated $1.50–$3.00 per 1,000 views, while comedy sits at the low end around $0.30–$0.80.

Work a quick example. A creator averaging **2 million qualified views a month** would earn an estimated **$1,000 to $4,000 monthly** from Creator Rewards alone — about **$12,000 to $48,000 a year** — before brand deals, [TikTok Shop](/blog/new-tiktok-monetization-features-2026/), or LIVE gifts. If a ban removed US access, that entire stream would stop the day the app went dark. ## What Happens to Your Balances and Payouts If TikTok Is Banned

A ban would not just stop future earnings — it could strand money you have already earned but not yet withdrawn. This is the most overlooked risk, and the easiest to defend against.

- **LIVE gift diamonds.** Creators keep roughly 50% of gift value, and diamonds convert at about $0.005 each (200 diamonds ≈ $1). Withdrawals use a tiered minimum: **$1 (200 diamonds)** for newer creators and **$10 (2,000 diamonds)** for regular accounts, with processing that typically takes **5 to 15 business days and can run up to 21**. A large diamond balance sitting in the app is money at risk during any disruption.
- **Creator Rewards.** Rewards are calculated and paid on a monthly cycle. An accrued balance below the payout threshold could be left in limbo if the app became unavailable mid-cycle.
- **TikTok Shop.** Shop affiliate and seller payments run on a biweekly schedule with a 5% standard affiliate commission and a 2.9% platform fee, so there is almost always some in-flight balance.

The practical takeaway: **withdraw available balances on a regular cadence** instead of letting them accumulate. Cashing out monthly turns a potential total loss into, at worst, a few weeks of exposure.

## Common Mistakes Creators Make About the Ban

The biggest costs around the TikTok ban usually come from overreacting or misreading the story, not from the ban itself.

1. **Treating every extension as "the ban is over."** Each deadline that passes is a pause, not a resolution. The law is still valid and the divestiture is still unfinished.
2. **Panic-quitting a platform that is paying you.** TikTok revenue has stayed strong through the uncertainty. Abandoning a paying channel on speculation is a guaranteed loss taken to avoid a possible one.
3. **Letting balances pile up.** Undrawn diamonds and pending payouts are the part of your earnings most exposed to a disruption. Withdraw regularly.
4. **Having no audience you own.** If your entire relationship with your followers lives inside one app, a ban — or just an algorithm change — can sever it instantly.
5. **Trusting viral rumors over reporting.** Creators speculating about the ban generate more noise than signal. Follow tech-policy journalists and official pages instead.

1. **Capture your audience off-platform.** Build an email list, a website, or a community you own. This is the one asset no platform decision can take from you.

4. **Withdraw on a schedule.** Cash out diamonds and payouts at a fixed cadence to minimize stranded balances.
