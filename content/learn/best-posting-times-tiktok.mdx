The best time to post on TikTok is generally **7:00-9:00 AM or 7:00-10:00 PM in your audience's local timezone**, when commute and evening scrolling put the most of your viewers online. Published industry datasets — including [Hootsuite's 2026 analysis](https://blog.hootsuite.com/tiktok-algorithm/) — point to Thursday mornings and Saturday midday as unusually strong windows, but every published chart reflects its own dataset, so the times in this guide are starting points to test against your own TikTok analytics, not algorithmic guarantees. This guide gives you hour-by-hour starting points for US, UK, and Australian audiences, niche-specific windows, the algorithm mechanics that make timing matter, and a five-step method to find your personal peak window.

## Why Posting Time Affects TikTok Reach

Posting time shapes the critical first hours of a video's life. According to [TikTok's official explanation of the For You feed](https://newsroom.tiktok.com/en-us/how-tiktok-recommends-videos-for-you), the recommendation system weighs three groups of signals: **user interactions** (likes, comments, shares, and especially watch time and completion) carry the most weight, **video information** (captions, sounds, hashtags) carries medium weight, and **device and account settings** carry the least. Notably, TikTok states that follower count and past video performance are *not* direct ranking factors — every video is evaluated on its own engagement. That newsroom post dates to June 2020, so treat it as the source for core mechanics rather than for recent changes.

Creators commonly observe that a new video is first shown to a small initial audience — often described as roughly 300-500 viewers — and expands in waves when that group engages well. TikTok does not publish exact batch sizes, so treat those figures as observed heuristics rather than confirmed mechanics; [how the TikTok algorithm works](/learn/tiktok-algorithm-explained/) covers the distribution model in depth.

Timing connects to this system in one simple way: posting when your audience is active means your earliest viewers are people likely to watch, finish, and share — the exact signals the algorithm weights most heavily. Posting at 3:00 AM for a US audience does not "penalize" your video, but it hands the first-hour test to whoever happens to be awake, and a weak first-hour engagement profile slows expansion. Timing does not rescue weak content, and strong content can still travel from an off-peak slot; timing simply stacks the early odds in your favor.

## Best Posting Times for US Audiences

US audience activity follows predictable rhythms tied to commutes, school schedules, lunch breaks, and evening leisure. ## Does Posting Time Affect TikTok Earnings?

Posting time affects earnings indirectly but measurably, because monetized views on TikTok are engagement-gated. The [Creator Rewards Program](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/creator-rewards-program/creator-rewards-program) only pays for **qualified views**: unique For You feed views watched for more than 5 seconds on original videos over 1 minute, with each video needing 1,000 qualified views before it earns anything. A view that scrolls past in 2 seconds is worth $0. Posting when your audience is attentive raises the share of views that clear the 5-second bar and improves the completion signals that drive further distribution.

The arithmetic makes the stakes concrete. The program pays $0.50-$2.00 per 1,000 qualified views in the US market. A creator generating 200,000 qualified views per month earns an estimated $100-$400; if better timing and stronger hooks lifted that to 300,000 qualified views, the estimate rises to $150-$600. That is a hypothetical illustration, not a measured result — but it shows why timing work compounds: it feeds both the algorithm's distribution decisions and the qualified-view count your payouts are built on. The same logic applies to engagement rate, which is the metric brands screen before sponsoring you; our guide on [how to increase your engagement rate](/learn/increase-engagement-rate/) covers the other levers.

## How to Find Your Personal Best Posting Time in 5 Steps

Published tables are averages; your analytics are ground truth. TikTok gives every business and creator account the data needed to find your actual peak windows.

1. **Open your follower activity data.** In TikTok analytics, go to the Followers tab and find the "Most active times" chart, which shows when your specific followers are online by hour and day. Our [TikTok analytics guide](/learn/tiktok-analytics-guide/) walks through where each report lives and what it means.
2. **Map your top posts against that chart.** Pull your 20 best-performing videos from the past 90 days, note their exact posting times, and check how many landed inside high-activity windows. Overlap suggests timing is already working for you; scatter suggests content is carrying the load alone.
3. **Run a two-week timing experiment.** Post comparable content at your hypothesized best time and at two alternates, keeping format and topic consistent so time is the main variable. One round of tests is weather, three rounds is climate — repeat before you conclude anything.
4. **Judge results at 24 hours, not 1 hour.** Some videos spike late as distribution waves expand. Compare views, average watch time, and completion rate at the 24-hour mark for a fair read.
5. **Account for your timezone mix.** If your followers split across regions — say 60/40 US East/West — one post cannot hit both peaks. Test a mid-morning ET slot that bridges both coasts, or post twice daily during each region's window and compare.

## Building a Consistent Posting Schedule

Consistency compounds the value of good timing. A predictable schedule keeps your output aligned with your audience's rhythm, gives you clean comparison data (videos posted at the same time each day are easier to evaluate against each other), and builds the viewing habit that turns casual viewers into followers. How often you post interacts with when you post — our data on the [posting frequency and engagement correlation](/learn/posting-frequency-engagement-correlation/) shows how cadence relates to per-post performance.

Three practices make a schedule sustainable:

- **Start with 3-5 posts per week** at your identified best times, using TikTok's built-in scheduler so a busy day does not break the pattern. Consistency at 4 posts per week beats bursts of 10 followed by silence.
- **Batch your production.** Filming and editing several videos in one session decouples creation from publishing, so you can hit peak windows without creating in real time. The full workflow is in our guide to [batch content creation for TikTok](/learn/tiktok-batch-content-creation/).
- **Plan around content pillars.** Defined [content pillars](/learn/tiktok-content-pillars/) let you match content types to their best windows — a food creator might schedule recipes for the 5:00 PM dinner-decision slot and behind-the-scenes content for late evening.

Re-evaluate monthly. Audience behavior shifts with seasons, school calendars, and daylight-saving changes, so the window that worked in January deserves a re-test in July.

## Common Posting-Time Mistakes

Most timing problems come from misreading what posting time can and cannot do:

1. **Posting in your own timezone instead of your audience's.** A London creator with a US-majority audience who posts at 8:00 PM UK time is publishing into the mid-afternoon US lull. Always schedule against the audience map in your analytics.
2. **Copying universal charts without testing.** Every published dataset — including this one and Hootsuite's — describes its own sample. Treat charts as hypotheses; treat your analytics as the verdict.
3. **Blaming timing for content problems.** If videos underperform at every hour, the issue is hook, watch time, or topic — the signals the algorithm actually ranks on. No posting slot fixes a 20% completion rate.
4. **Judging a slot after one post.** Single videos vary enormously for reasons unrelated to timing. Test each window with at least three comparable posts before keeping or discarding it.
5. **Deleting and reposting at a "better" time.** Deleting a video that is mid-distribution resets its accumulated engagement signals. Let it run 24-48 hours; apply the timing lesson to the next post instead.
6. **Chasing the peak into a crowded queue.** The busiest window has the most viewers and the most competing posts. If you are consistently drowned out at 8:00 PM, the shoulder slots (6:00-7:00 PM or 9:00-10:00 PM) can produce a better engaged-viewers-to-competition ratio.

## FAQ

### What is the best time to post on TikTok?

The best general windows are **7:00-9:00 AM and 7:00-10:00 PM in your audience's local timezone**, with Hootsuite's 2026 brand-account data highlighting Thursday mornings and Saturday midday. Your own follower-activity chart in TikTok analytics beats any published table.

### Is it bad to post on TikTok at night?

No — off-peak posting slows the first-hour engagement test but does not penalize the video. TikTok evaluates each video on its interaction signals, and strong content posted at 2:00 AM can still expand once active viewers find it. Peak windows simply improve the odds of a strong start.

### How many times a day should you post on TikTok?

One well-made video per day is a stronger baseline than several rushed ones, because watch time and completion outweigh raw volume in the ranking signals. Creators averaging around 20 posts per month hit a sustainable cadence; add a second daily slot only if your audience spans multiple timezones.

### Does posting time affect Creator Rewards earnings?

Indirectly, yes. The Creator Rewards Program pays $0.50-$2.00 per 1,000 qualified views — unique For You feed views watched over 5 seconds — so posting when your audience is attentive raises the share of views that qualify and the completion signals that expand distribution.

### Does follower count decide how many views your videos get?

No. TikTok states that follower count and past video performance are not direct ranking factors in For You feed recommendations. Each video is tested on its own engagement, which is why timing your post to reach engaged viewers early matters at every account size.
