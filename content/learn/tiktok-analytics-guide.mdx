The TikTok analytics dashboard is free for every Business or Creator account and organizes your performance data into four tabs — Overview, Content, Followers, and LIVE — covering views, watch time, traffic sources, engagement, and audience demographics. Reading those four tabs correctly is the difference between guessing what works and running a data-driven strategy, because the same signals that predict reach also determine what your content can earn.

## What the TikTok Analytics Dashboard Tells You (And Why It Matters)

TikTok provides analytics to every account that switches to a Business or Creator account — a free change that takes seconds in your settings. Once enabled, you get rolling performance data across 7-day, 28-day, and 60-day windows (plus custom date ranges) in four primary tabs: Overview, Content, Followers, and LIVE.

The dashboard is not a vanity-metrics display. It shows which content formats hold attention, when your followers are actually online, and which traffic sources drive your views. That data directly informs your [content strategy](/learn/tiktok-content-pillars/) and helps you [optimize posting times](/learn/best-posting-times-tiktok/) around your own audience rather than generic "best time to post" charts.

It also matters for money. TikTok's [Creator Rewards Program](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/creator-rewards-program/creator-rewards-program) pays on *qualified* views — unique For You feed views watched for more than 5 seconds, excluding paid, fraudulent, and "not interested" views — so metrics like watch time and traffic source are not abstract. They are the inputs to your paycheck.

To access your dashboard: tap the three-line menu on your profile, select "TikTok Studio" or "Creator tools," then "Analytics." On desktop, TikTok Studio offers an expanded view with downloadable data.

## The Overview Tab: Your Account Health Snapshot

The Overview tab presents top-line performance across your selected time frame. It is your starting point for spotting trends before you drill into individual videos.

### Video Views

Total video views is the aggregate number of times your content was played during the period. A single viewer replaying a video counts each play, so raw views overstate unique reach. Note the gap between this number and the qualified-view standard used for monetization: for Creator Rewards, only unique For You feed views watched longer than 5 seconds count toward earnings.

### Profile Views

Profile views count users who visited your profile page — a direct indicator of curiosity, since these viewers saw something compelling enough to want more. If profile views are climbing but followers are flat, the problem is usually your bio, profile picture, or pinned videos rather than your content.

### Likes, Comments, Shares, and Saves

These appear as totals and trend lines. Individually they are noisy; combined, they form your engagement rate, which is the metric brands and the algorithm both care about. The canonical formula:

**Engagement rate = (Likes + Comments + Shares + Saves) ÷ Views × 100**

Here is how to interpret the result, based on the view-based formula used across our calculators:

| Engagement Rate | Rating | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| 10%+ | Excellent | Top-tier content-audience fit |
| 5–10% | Good | Above the platform average |
| 2–5% | Average | Platform average is 4.07% |
| Under 2% | Below average | Content-audience mismatch likely |

Brands typically look for at least 4% engagement before sponsoring a creator, and 6%+ commands premium rates. Expect your baseline to shift with size: nano accounts (1K–10K followers) average 8–15%, micro accounts (10K–50K) 5–8%, mid-tier (50K–200K) 3–5%, macro (200K–1M) 2–4%, and mega accounts (1M+) 1–3%. Run your own numbers with the [engagement rate calculator](/calculators/engagement-rate/).

Shares deserve special attention because they push content beyond your existing audience, and saves signal long-term value — both feed into [how the algorithm ranks your content](/learn/tiktok-algorithm-explained/).

### Followers

The follower trend line shows net growth (new followers minus unfollows). Spikes usually correlate with a video that broke out; dips often coincide with posting gaps or off-topic content. Watch the trend direction over weeks, not the daily wiggle.

## The Content Tab: Video-Level Performance Breakdown

The Content tab shifts you from account-level trends to individual video analysis. Tap into any video for its deep-dive metrics.

### Watch Time and Average Watch Duration

Average watch time is arguably the single most important metric in your analytics. TikTok's [official explanation of its recommendation system](https://newsroom.tiktok.com/en-us/how-tiktok-recommends-videos-for-you) (published June 2020, and still the clearest first-party statement of how the For You feed works) says that whether a viewer finishes a longer video is a strong indicator of interest — weighted more heavily than lighter signals like whether the viewer and creator are in the same country.

TikTok does not publish official watch-time benchmarks, but these are reasonable working targets creators commonly aim for, based on the mechanics of shorter videos being easier to finish:

| Video Length | Working Target (Avg. Watch %) |
|---|---|
| Under 15 seconds | 65%+ |
| 15–30 seconds | 50%+ |
| 30–60 seconds | 40%+ |
| 1–3 minutes | 25%+ |
| 3+ minutes | 15%+ |

Treat these as directional, not official. The comparison that actually matters is your own videos against each other: if your top video held 55% and your bottom video held 20%, study the difference in hooks and pacing. Track this alongside the [completion rate calculator](/calculators/completion-rate/).

Watch time matters doubly for monetization: Creator Rewards only pays on videos longer than 60 seconds, so holding attention past the one-minute mark is a revenue skill, not just an algorithm skill. See our guide on [short vs. long TikTok videos](/learn/short-vs-long-tiktok-videos/) for how to make the trade-off.

### Traffic Sources

Traffic sources reveal where views come from. The five main sources:

- **For You feed:** Views from TikTok's main recommendation feed. For most growing accounts, this should be your largest source — and it is the only source that produces qualified views for Creator Rewards.
- **Following feed:** Views from existing followers. If this dominates, your content is not breaking out to new audiences.
- **Profile page:** Views from users browsing your profile — a discoverability and curiosity signal.
- **Search:** Views from TikTok search. Growing search traffic means your [TikTok SEO strategy](/learn/tiktok-seo-search-ranking/) is working, and search has become a genuinely meaningful discovery channel.
- **Sounds:** Views from users browsing a sound page. Relevant mainly if you use trending audio.

### Video Completion Rate

Completion rate is the percentage of viewers who watched to the end. It directly influences whether distribution expands. Shorter videos naturally complete at higher rates, so always compare completion within similar video lengths, not across them.

## The Followers Tab: Audience Demographics and Activity

The Followers tab unlocks at 100 followers and provides the demographic data that should shape both your content and your rate card.

### Gender and Age Distribution

Follower demographics appear as percentage breakdowns by gender and age bracket (13–17 through 55+). This matters for two reasons: it tells you whether you are reaching your intended audience, and it directly affects your [brand deal rates](/calculators/brand-deal-rate/) — advertisers generally pay more for engaged audiences in prime purchasing demographics.

### Top Territories

Geographic data shows which countries your followers live in — and this is one of the most financially consequential numbers on the entire dashboard. Ad-driven payouts vary sharply by region. Using US earnings as the 1.0 baseline, our regional model applies these multipliers:

| Region | Earnings Multiplier vs. US |
|---|---|
| United States | 1.0 |
| United Kingdom | 0.9 |
| Canada | 0.85 |
| Australia / NZ | 0.8 |
| European Union | 0.75 |
| Asia-Pacific | 0.6 |
| Latin America | 0.5 |

Two creators with identical view counts can earn very different amounts purely because of audience geography. Factor your territory mix into any [earnings estimate](/calculators/tiktok-money/).

### Follower Activity Times

This section shows when your followers are active, by day and hour, based on their actual behavior rather than global averages. Cross-reference it with your [posting schedule](/learn/best-posting-times-tiktok/) — early engagement velocity on a fresh post is commonly observed to help initial distribution, though TikTok does not publish exact mechanics.

## LIVE Analytics: Real-Time Stream Performance

If you go LIVE (which requires 1,000 followers and age 16+; receiving gifts requires 18+), a dedicated tab tracks stream performance. Key metrics:

- **Total views and unique viewers** per stream
- **New followers** gained during sessions
- **Total LIVE time** across the period
- **Diamonds** — the virtual currency you receive from gifts

Diamonds convert to real money at a fixed rate: 1 diamond = $0.005, so 200 diamonds = $1. That works out to roughly a 50% revenue share on what viewers spend, since viewers buy coins at about $0.0106 each and 2 coins convert to 1 diamond. A stream that brings in 10,000 diamonds is worth about $50; 100,000 diamonds is about $500.

It helps to know what the gifts behind those diamonds actually cost. Viewers buy gifts in coins, and creators keep roughly half the spend:

| Gift | Viewer Cost | Approx. Creator Share |
|---|---|---|
| Rose | 1 coin (~$0.01) | ~$0.005 |
| Gold Mine | 1,000 coins (~$10.60) | ~$5.30 |
| Universe | 34,999 coins (~$370.99) | ~$185.50 |

A single Universe gift puts about $185 in your pocket — the same as roughly 37,000 Roses — which is why a handful of high-value gifters can outweigh a large audience of light tippers, and why your LIVE tab is worth reading gift-by-gift, not just as a diamond total. Withdrawals require a $1 minimum for new creators ($10 for regular accounts) and typically take 5–15 business days. ## The Metrics That Actually Move the Number

Not all metrics are equal. TikTok's official documentation groups ranking signals into three tiers: user interactions (watch time, completion, shares, likes, comments — the strongest group), video information (captions, sounds, hashtags), and device/account settings (the weakest). Notably, TikTok states that follower count and past video performance are **not** direct ranking factors — every video gets evaluated on its own signals.

That hierarchy tells you where to focus:

| Priority | Metric | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Average watch time | Strongest interest signal per TikTok's own docs |
| 2 | Completion rate | Finishing a video is an explicit interest indicator |
| 3 | Share rate | Pushes content beyond your audience |
| 4 | Save rate | Signals reference-worthy, evergreen value |
| 5 | Comment rate | Community engagement and session time |
| 6 | Follower conversion | Measures profile effectiveness, not reach |

Fix watch time and completion first. Everything else compounds on top of them.

## From Analytics to Dollars: Three Worked Examples

Analytics only matter if they connect to outcomes. Here is how the numbers on your dashboard translate into earnings, using published program rates. All figures are estimates based on TikTok's official documentation and published RPM ranges — never guarantees.

**Example 1 — Creator Rewards.** The program requires 10,000 followers, 100,000 video views in the last 30 days, age 18+, and a personal account in good standing in an eligible country. Only original videos over 60 seconds earn, and only after reaching 1,000 qualified For You feed views. US payouts run roughly $0.50–$2.00 per 1,000 qualified views. If your Content tab shows 500,000 monthly views and roughly 60% came from the For You feed with strong watch time, you might have ~300,000 qualified views — an estimated $150–$600 for the month. Niche shifts this range: our modeled estimates put finance content at $1.50–$3.00 RPM, education at $0.80–$2.00, and entertainment/gaming at $0.40–$1.00. (For context, the legacy Creator Fund paid just $0.02–$0.05 per 1,000 views — Creator Rewards pays 10-40x more.)

**Example 2 — Engagement rate and brand deals.** A micro creator (10K–50K followers) typically commands $200–$1,000 per sponsored post. Where you land in that range depends heavily on the engagement rate your Overview tab documents: a creator at 7% engagement can justify the top of the band, while one at 2% will struggle to justify the bottom. Screenshot-ready analytics are your negotiating leverage — see the [brand deal rate calculator](/calculators/brand-deal-rate/) for tier-by-tier rates.

**Example 3 — LIVE gifting.** Your LIVE tab shows diamonds per stream. Eight streams a month averaging 5,000 diamonds each equals 40,000 diamonds — about $200 at $0.005 per diamond. If analytics show one stream format consistently doubles your diamonds-per-hour, that single insight is worth more than any generic streaming advice.

## How to Turn Analytics Into a Weekly Action Plan

A 20-minute weekly review beats hours of unfocused scrolling through charts.

**Step 1: Compare your top 3 and bottom 3 videos from the past 7 days.** Look at hooks, topics, lengths, and posting times. Patterns in what your audience rewards emerge quickly.

**Step 2: Check follower activity times.** Adjust your posting schedule if active hours have shifted — they change seasonally and as your audience grows.

**Step 3: Examine traffic source trends.** A falling For You percentage means weakening algorithmic distribution — revisit hooks, and check the [For You feed eligibility issues](/learn/tiktok-shadowban-fix/) that suppress reach. Rising search traffic means you should double down on [search-optimized content](/learn/tiktok-seo-search-ranking/).

**Step 4: Track your [engagement rate](/calculators/engagement-rate/) week over week.** Some decline as followers grow is normal; a sharp drop signals content-audience misalignment.

**Step 5: Document everything in a simple spreadsheet.** Weekly views, engagement rate, best-performing format, follower growth. Patterns that are invisible in a single session become obvious over 4–8 weeks.

## Common Analytics Mistakes That Waste the Data

**Chasing raw views instead of watch time.** A 200K-view video with 3-second average watch time did less for your account than a 20K-view video watched to completion — and earned less too, since sub-5-second views never qualify for Creator Rewards.

**Comparing against the wrong benchmark.** Published "average engagement rate" figures vary wildly because they measure different populations with different formulas. Our canonical 4.07% platform average uses the view-based formula including saves; benchmarks computed per-follower, or measured only on brand accounts, will read much lower. Know which formula a benchmark uses before you panic.

**Reacting to single-video variance.** One flop is noise. Three consecutive videos underperforming on the same metric is a signal.

**Checking analytics without changing anything.** The dashboard is only useful if each review ends with one concrete adjustment — a new hook style, a different posting slot, a longer format to test with [A/B testing](/learn/tiktok-ab-testing-content/).

**Ignoring the qualified-view gap.** Creators regularly overestimate earnings by applying RPM to total views. Only unique For You feed views over 5 seconds qualify — your Following-feed and profile views pay nothing under Creator Rewards.

## FAQ

### What is a good engagement rate on TikTok?

Using the view-based formula (likes + comments + shares + saves ÷ views × 100), the platform average is 4.07%. Anything above 5% is good, 10%+ is excellent, and under 2% signals a content-audience mismatch. Brands generally want 4%+ before sponsoring, with 6%+ earning premium rates.

### What counts as a qualified view in the Creator Rewards Program?

A qualified view is a unique For You feed view watched for more than 5 seconds. Paid-promotion views, fraudulent views, and views where the user tapped "not interested" are excluded, and a video must reach 1,000 qualified views before it starts earning, per [TikTok's official Creator Rewards documentation](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/creator-rewards-program/creator-rewards-program).

### Does follower count affect how many views my videos get?

Not directly. TikTok's official newsroom post on the recommendation system states that neither follower count nor past video performance are direct ranking factors. Each video is distributed based on its own engagement signals — which is why small accounts can go viral and large accounts can flop.

### How often should I check TikTok analytics?

Once per week for a structured 20-minute review is the sweet spot for most creators. Daily checking encourages overreaction to normal variance; monthly checking lets problems compound. The exception: check individual video performance 24–48 hours after posting to catch patterns while the context is fresh.

### How much does TikTok pay per 1,000 views?

Under the current Creator Rewards Program, roughly $0.50–$2.00 per 1,000 *qualified* views in the US market, varying by niche, region, and engagement quality. The legacy Creator Fund paid $0.02–$0.05 per 1,000 views. These are estimates from published ranges, not guarantees — see the [TikTok money calculator](/calculators/tiktok-money/) for a personalized model.

### Why do my analytics only go back 60 days?

TikTok retains dashboard data for a rolling window (up to 60 days for most metrics). If you want a longer history, export or record your key metrics in a spreadsheet during your weekly review — there is no way to recover data older than the retention window.
