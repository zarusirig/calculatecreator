TikTok completion rate — the percentage of viewers who watch a video from start to finish — falls sharply as videos get longer, dropping from roughly 89% for 7-second clips to under 10% for 10-minute videos. Completion rate is one of the algorithm's strongest ranking signals, which makes it arguably more important than likes or comments for determining how widely your content is distributed. As a benchmark, the industry convention treats 80%+ completion as excellent, 60-80% as good, 40-60% as average, and below 40% as weak — and distribution reliably improves once a video clears about 60% completion.

## Key Findings

Completion rate is among the most influential factors in TikTok's recommendation engine. When a viewer watches an entire video and loops back to watch again, the system reads that as a strong quality signal and pushes the content to larger audiences.

Here are representative completion-rate benchmarks by video length for 2026:

| Video Length | Est. Completion Rate | Est. Loop Rate | Distribution Signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| 7 seconds | ~89% | ~42% | Excellent completion, low total watch time |
| 15 seconds | ~73% | ~28% | Best balance of completion and watch time |
| 30 seconds | ~55% | ~15% | Average completion, solid watch time |
| 60 seconds | ~38% | ~6% | Below-benchmark completion, high watch time |
| 90 seconds | ~28% | ~3% | Below-benchmark completion |
| 3 minutes | ~17% | ~1% | Low completion, very high watch time |
| 5 minutes | ~12% | ~0.4% | Low completion, very high watch time |
| 10 minutes | ~8% | ~0.1% | Low completion, maximum watch time |

Map those numbers onto the canonical benchmark bands and the picture is clear: only videos under about 20 seconds routinely reach the "excellent" (80%+) or "good" (60-80%) completion tiers, while anything past 30 seconds sits in "average" territory or below on a completion basis. That does not make longer videos worse — it means longer videos win on a different metric (total watch time) rather than on completion percentage.

The critical insight: **15-second videos hit the sweet spot** between completion rate and watch time. Seven-second clips finish at a higher percentage, but 15-second videos generate more total watch time per view (about 10.9 seconds of average watch time versus roughly 6.3 seconds for 7-second clips), and the algorithm weighs total watch time alongside completion rate.

Three patterns emerge from the data:

1. **Every additional 15 seconds of length reduces completion rate by roughly 12-18 percentage points** in the sub-60-second range.
2. **Loop rate (viewers rewatching) drops faster than completion rate**, so short-form content benefits disproportionately from rewatch behavior.
3. **Longer videos compensate with higher total watch time**, which is why TikTok has not abandoned long-form — a 3-minute video at ~17% completion still delivers about 30 seconds of average watch time per view.

## TikTok Video Completion Rate by Video Length — Primary Data

Understanding what drives completion rate requires looking past the raw percentages. The relationship between length and completion is not linear — it follows a decay curve with predictable drop-off points every creator should know.

### 15s

Fifteen-second videos remain the backbone of high-performing TikTok content in 2026. At an estimated ~73% completion, they are watched to the end by nearly three of four viewers and land squarely in the "good" benchmark band.

**Why 15 seconds works so well:**

- **Attention commitment is low.** Viewers will invest 15 seconds even when the content does not immediately hook them, which means fewer early drop-offs.
- **Loop behavior multiplies watch time.** A ~28% loop rate means more than one in four viewers watch the video at least twice — often without consciously deciding to, because the clip restarts before they swipe away.
- **Ideal for single-idea content.** One tip, one joke, one reveal. The constraint forces you to distill the video to its most engaging essence.

Completion rate for 15-second videos varies meaningfully by niche. The figures below are representative estimates, not exact population values:

| Niche | Est. 15s Completion | Est. 15s Loop Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Comedy/Skits | ~78% | ~36% |
| Dance/Music | ~77% | ~34% |
| Quick Tips/Hacks | ~74% | ~29% |
| Product Reviews | ~70% | ~23% |
| Storytelling | ~67% | ~20% |

Comedy and dance content benefits from the "punchline effect" — viewers rewatch to catch the joke or appreciate the choreography, driving loop rates above one in three. Educational quick tips also perform strongly because the entire value proposition fits inside the 15-second window.

### 30s

Thirty-second videos are the transition zone between ultra-short and mid-length content. At an estimated ~55% completion they lose roughly a quarter of the audience compared to 15-second clips, but they offer substantially more room for narrative development, landing at the top of the "average" band.

**The 30-second drop-off pattern:**

Most 30-second videos lose viewers at two predictable points — around the 8-10 second mark (the initial hook window) and again around 20-22 seconds (where viewers decide whether the payoff is worth the remaining time). Creators who structure the video with a hook at 0-3 seconds, development at 10-20 seconds, and a payoff at 25-30 seconds tend to see completion rates several points above average.

The estimated ~15% loop rate for 30-second content is meaningful: with partial replays factored in, these videos accumulate roughly 17 seconds of average watch time per view, making them competitive with 60-second videos in total watch time generated.

For creators who want to see how completion feeds their [overall engagement rate](/data/engagement/avg-engagement-rate-follower-count-2026/), the correlation is strong — videos in the top quartile for completion consistently earn far more total engagements than videos in the bottom quartile, because the same content quality that holds attention also earns saves, shares, and comments.

## TikTok Video Completion Rate by Video Length — Extended Data

### 60-Second and 90-Second Videos

The one-minute mark is a significant psychological threshold. At an estimated ~38% completion, 60-second videos lose nearly two-thirds of their audience before the end and fall just below the "average" band on completion. This tier has, however, seen steady improvement in recent years as audiences have grown more accustomed to longer TikTok content — the completion figures for 60-second videos have edged upward rather than declined.

Ninety-second videos average an estimated ~28% completion and occupy a tricky middle ground. They are too long to benefit from the loop-and-rewatch dynamics that power short-form, but too short to deliver the depth that justifies a longer viewing session. Most creators should either trim to 60 seconds or expand to 3 minutes rather than target the 90-second range.

### Long-Form: 3 Minutes to 10 Minutes

TikTok's push into long-form has produced a distinct viewing pattern. Three-minute videos average an estimated ~17% completion — roughly one in six viewers finishes. That sounds low until you contextualize it by total watch time: a 3-minute video with 100,000 views at ~17% completion generates roughly 50,000 minutes of total watch time. A 15-second video would need about 280,000 views to match that figure.

| Duration | Est. Watch Time Per View | Views Needed for 50K Min Watch Time |
|---|---|---|
| 15 seconds | ~10.9 sec | ~275,000 |
| 30 seconds | ~17.1 sec | ~175,000 |
| 60 seconds | ~28.4 sec | ~106,000 |
| 3 minutes | ~30.4 sec | ~99,000 |
| 5 minutes | ~42.6 sec | ~70,000 |
| 10 minutes | ~61.8 sec | ~49,000 |

This is why TikTok incentivizes longer content through the [Creator Rewards Program](/calculators/tiktok-creator-fund/), which pays $0.50-$2.00 per 1,000 qualified views and requires videos longer than 60 seconds: longer videos generate more total watch time per view, which keeps users on the platform longer. Note the eligibility trade-off — the sub-60-second clips that complete best do not qualify for Creator Rewards at all, so the "best completion rate" length and the "best monetization" length are often not the same video.

## Methodology

The figures on this page are representative benchmark estimates, not the output of a proprietary study. They are modeled and synthesized from publicly reported third-party analytics, industry benchmark publications, and TikTok's own creator documentation, then calibrated to TT Calculator's canonical completion-rate bands (excellent 80%+, good 60-80%, average 40-60%, below average under 40%).

**How to read these numbers:**

- **Completion rate definition:** the percentage of viewers who watch from the first frame to the final frame. TikTok counts a complete view when the progress bar reaches 100%. Partial replays and loops are tracked separately.
- **Loop rate definition:** the percentage of viewers who watch the video more than once in a single session, i.e. sessions exceeding 100% watch-through.
- **Estimate framing:** completion rate is not publicly visible on videos — it can only be read from your own analytics dashboard. Because there is no public population dataset, every percentage here should be treated as a directional benchmark for comparison, not an exact measured value. Your real numbers depend on your niche, hook quality, and audience.
- **Where your own data lives:** you can find your completion and average-watch-time figures inside your creator analytics. TikTok's [official Business and Creator analytics documentation](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/creator-and-business-accounts/understanding-your-analytics) is the authoritative source for locating them. For how these interaction signals are defined and counted platform-wide, [Hootsuite's 2026 engagement-rate guide](https://blog.hootsuite.com/calculate-engagement-rate/) is a useful reference.

Treat the benchmarks as a map, not a scoreboard: they tell you roughly where a well-made video of a given length should land, so you can spot when your own content is materially under- or over-performing for its duration.

If your completion rates sit below the averages for your duration, these data-backed strategies help:

- **Front-load value.** The first 2-3 seconds decide whether viewers commit. Open with visual movement, a provocative question, or on-screen text that creates curiosity.
- **Match length to content type.** Forcing a 15-second idea into 60 seconds destroys completion rate. If you can deliver the value faster, cut it shorter.
- **Use pattern interrupts.** In videos over 30 seconds, change the visual pattern every 5-8 seconds — switch angles, add text overlays, change scenes. Each interrupt resets the viewer's attention clock.
- **Test and iterate.** Post the same concept at different lengths and compare completion in your analytics before committing to a format.

## FAQ

### What is a good completion rate on TikTok?

By industry convention, 80%+ completion is excellent, 60-80% is good, 40-60% is average, and below 40% is weak — but those bands assume a mix of lengths. Completion depends heavily on duration: a 15-second video should comfortably clear 60-70%, while a 3-minute video at 17% can still be a strong performer because it generates far more total watch time. Always judge completion against your video's length, not against a single universal number.

### What completion rate does the TikTok algorithm reward?

Distribution reliably improves once a video clears roughly 60% completion, and completion is one of the strongest signals the recommendation system uses. TikTok does not publish exact ranking weights, so treat the 60% figure as an observed threshold rather than a confirmed rule. Total watch time matters alongside completion, which is why longer videos with lower completion can still be pushed widely.

### Why do shorter TikTok videos have higher completion rates?

Shorter videos ask less of the viewer, so fewer people drop off before the end, and they restart quickly enough to rack up loops. A 7-second clip finishing near 89% is mostly a function of low time commitment. The trade-off is total watch time: a short clip that finishes at 89% still only delivers a few seconds of watch time per view, whereas a longer video can deliver far more even at a low completion percentage.

### Is 15 seconds really the best video length for TikTok?

For most creators optimizing the balance of completion rate and total watch time, yes — 15-second videos combine a high (~73%) completion rate with meaningful watch time and strong loop behavior. But "best" depends on your goal: comedy peaks at 15-20 seconds, education often needs 45-90 seconds, and anything you want monetized through Creator Rewards must exceed 60 seconds regardless of completion.

### Can I see my own completion rate on TikTok?

Yes, but only in your own analytics — completion rate is never shown publicly on a video. Open your creator analytics dashboard and review each video's average watch time and the percentage of viewers who watched the full clip. TikTok's official analytics documentation explains where to find these figures, and our [completion rate calculator](/calculators/completion-rate/) helps you translate average watch time into a completion percentage you can benchmark.

### How do I increase my TikTok completion rate?

Match each video's length to its idea and front-load the payoff. Open with movement, a question, or on-screen text in the first 2-3 seconds so viewers commit, then cut anything that pads the runtime. In videos over 30 seconds, add a visual pattern interrupt — an angle change, scene cut, or text overlay — every 5-8 seconds to reset attention. The single biggest lever is not forcing a 15-second idea into 60 seconds: if you can deliver the value faster, cut it shorter and completion climbs.
