# Audience Journey Mapping

name: audience-journey-mapping
description: Use when users mention audience segmentation, user journey, funnel mapping, user journey paths, audience personas, content funnel, user experience flow, or conversion path.

---

## Purpose

Audience journey mapping defines who visits the semantic content network, what they need at each stage of their journey, and how the content should guide them from initial awareness through decision and retention. This skill teaches agents how to segment audiences by knowledge level, map content to journey stages, design internal linking paths that serve each segment, and align content types to funnel positions. The result is a content network that serves every visitor appropriately, regardless of where they enter or how much they already know.

---

## Brand Identity Integration

When a Brand Identity Document exists, layer brand personality onto every journey stage:

- **Tone Calibration per Stage**: Each of the 5 journey stages (Problem Awareness → Solution Awareness → Solution Evaluation → Decision → Retention) gets a specific tone calibration from the Brand Voice & Tone Guide. Awareness content is typically warmer and more exploratory; Decision content is more direct and confident.
- **CTA Progression**: Map the CTA strategy from the Brand Identity Document's funnel map directly onto journey stages. Each stage has its own CTA type, copy template, and placement rule.
- **Messaging per Segment**: Each audience sophistication level (Novice/Intermediate/Advanced/Professional) uses vocabulary calibrated to both their knowledge level AND the brand voice. A "casual expert" brand talks to novices differently than an "academic authority" brand does.
- **Value Proposition Threading**: The brand's 3-5 value propositions should be woven through the journey — different propositions resonate at different stages. Map which value proposition is primary for each stage.
- **Social Proof Escalation**: Early journey stages use lighter social proof (community size, general testimonials); later stages use heavier proof (specific case studies, ROI numbers, named testimonials). Follow the Social Proof Template from the Brand Identity Document.

## Core Concepts

### Four Audience Segments

Every topic has visitors at different knowledge levels. The semantic content network must serve all of them. The standard segmentation:

**1. Novice (20-30% of audience)**
- Has little to no prior knowledge of the topic
- Searches using broad, basic queries
- Needs foundational definitions, explanations, and context
- Reads introductory content, beginner guides, and glossary-style pages
- Requires simple language, analogies, and step-by-step explanations
- Most likely to enter through high-volume, low-competition informational queries
- Highest bounce risk if content is too advanced

**2. Intermediate (40-50% of audience)**
- Has basic understanding and is looking to deepen knowledge
- Searches using more specific queries, often including qualifiers (best, how to, comparison)
- Needs detailed guides, comparisons, process explanations, and practical applications
- Comfortable with domain terminology but still needs context for specialized concepts
- This is the largest audience segment and should receive the most content investment
- Most likely to engage with multiple pages in a single session

**3. Advanced (15-25% of audience)**
- Has strong domain knowledge and is looking for expert-level information
- Searches using technical queries, specific entity names, and niche terminology
- Needs in-depth analysis, advanced techniques, edge cases, and nuanced discussions
- Expects precision and does not need basic concepts explained
- Likely to evaluate the site's authority based on depth and accuracy
- Lower volume but higher engagement and conversion potential

**4. Professional (5-10% of audience)**
- Works in the field or has professional-level expertise
- Searches for very specific data, research, tools, or solutions to professional challenges
- Needs original research, data sets, professional guides, and implementation resources
- Will immediately detect inaccuracy or superficiality
- Smallest segment but highest value per visitor
- Most likely to link to your content, cite it, or become a repeat visitor

### Five Journey Stages

Regardless of their knowledge level, visitors move through distinct stages in their relationship with a topic:

**Stage 1: Problem Awareness**
The visitor recognizes they have a problem, question, or need but has not yet defined it clearly. Content at this stage should:
- Help the visitor articulate their problem
- Provide broad overviews and context
- Use symptom-based and question-based language
- Focus on empathy and validation ("you're not alone in experiencing this")
- Link to more specific content that helps narrow the problem definition

**Stage 2: Solution Awareness**
The visitor understands their problem and is now aware that solutions exist. Content at this stage should:
- Present the landscape of possible solutions
- Explain different approaches, methodologies, or product categories
- Provide unbiased comparisons of solution types (not specific products yet)
- Help the visitor understand the criteria for choosing a solution
- Link to detailed evaluation content for each solution category

**Stage 3: Solution Evaluation**
The visitor is actively comparing specific solutions. Content at this stage should:
- Provide detailed comparisons of specific products, services, or approaches
- Include data-driven analysis, feature comparisons, and use-case matching
- Present pros and cons transparently
- Address common objections and concerns
- Link to decision-stage content (reviews, case studies, testimonials)

**Stage 4: Decision**
The visitor is ready to make a choice. Content at this stage should:
- Provide final validation and confidence-building information
- Include reviews, case studies, implementation guides, and getting-started resources
- Reduce friction in the decision process
- Offer clear calls to action
- Link to retention-stage content (setup guides, best practices)

**Stage 5: Retention**
The visitor has made their decision and is now a user, customer, or practitioner. Content at this stage should:
- Provide ongoing value through advanced tips, updates, and community resources
- Address post-decision questions and common challenges
- Build loyalty and advocacy
- Encourage return visits and deeper engagement with the content network
- Link back to evaluation content for related decisions

### 8-12 Journey Paths with Internal Linking

For each combination of audience segment and relevant journey stages, design a specific internal linking path -- a sequence of pages that guides the visitor through their journey. Each path should:

- **Have a clear entry point:** The page where visitors in that segment/stage are most likely to land (usually from search).
- **Follow a logical progression:** Each page links to the natural next page in the journey, moving the visitor closer to their goal.
- **Include 3-5 pages per path:** A typical journey path moves through 3-5 pages before the visitor either converts, leaves, or transitions to a different path.
- **Not dead-end:** Every page in a path must link forward to the next step. No page should be a dead end without clear next actions.
- **Cross-link between paths:** Where journeys intersect (e.g., an intermediate visitor in Solution Evaluation may need content designed for the Advanced Decision stage), provide cross-links between paths.

Example journey paths:
1. Novice Problem Awareness --> Novice Solution Awareness --> Intermediate Solution Evaluation --> Decision
2. Intermediate Solution Awareness --> Intermediate Solution Evaluation --> Decision --> Retention
3. Advanced Problem Awareness --> Advanced Solution Evaluation --> Decision
4. Professional Solution Evaluation --> Professional Decision --> Retention

Design 8-12 of these paths to cover the most common visitor scenarios. Each path becomes an internal linking architecture that the content network actively supports.

### Content Type Alignment Per Funnel Stage

Different types of content serve different stages of the funnel. The recommended distribution across the entire content network:

**70% Informative Content**
- Aligns with Problem Awareness and Solution Awareness stages
- Includes: guides, tutorials, explanations, definitions, overviews, how-to articles, educational content
- Targets informational search intent
- Builds topical authority and trust
- Generates the highest volume of organic traffic

**20% Commercial Content**
- Aligns with Solution Evaluation stage
- Includes: comparisons, reviews, best-of lists, feature analyses, buying guides, versus articles
- Targets commercial investigation intent
- Bridges the gap between learning and buying
- Moderate traffic volume but higher engagement

**10% Transactional Content**
- Aligns with Decision and Retention stages
- Includes: product pages, pricing pages, signup pages, implementation guides, onboarding resources
- Targets transactional and navigational intent
- Lowest traffic volume but highest conversion rate
- Should be the destination of journey paths, not the entry point

This 70/20/10 ratio ensures the content network has a strong informational foundation that feeds into commercial and transactional pages through well-designed journey paths.

### Entry Point Design Per Audience Level

Each audience segment enters the content network through different types of queries and pages. Design entry points intentionally:

**Novice entry points:**
- "What is [topic]" pages
- Beginner guides and introductions
- Glossary and definition pages
- "Complete guide to [topic] for beginners"

**Intermediate entry points:**
- "How to [specific task]" pages
- Comparison and best-of pages
- Process guides and tutorials
- "[Topic] tips and best practices"

**Advanced entry points:**
- Technical deep-dives and analysis pages
- Advanced technique and strategy pages
- Edge case and troubleshooting pages
- "[Topic] for [specific professional context]"

**Professional entry points:**
- Research and data pages
- Industry reports and analysis
- Tool and methodology pages
- Implementation and architecture guides

Each entry point page must be optimized not just for search performance but for its role as the beginning of a journey path. It must immediately communicate relevance to the target audience segment and provide clear next steps through internal linking.

## Implementation Guidance

When advising on audience journey mapping:

1. **Define the audience segments** for the specific topic. Adjust the default percentages based on the topic's nature (highly technical topics may have a larger Advanced/Professional share; consumer topics may skew more Novice/Intermediate).

2. **Map existing content** to segments and stages. Identify which cells in the segment-by-stage matrix are well-served and which have gaps.

3. **Design 8-12 journey paths** based on the most common visitor scenarios. Each path should have a named entry point, a sequence of 3-5 pages, and a clear destination.

4. **Assign content types** to each page in the content network based on its position in the funnel (informative, commercial, or transactional).

5. **Build the internal linking architecture** to support each journey path. Links should flow naturally from one page to the next in each path, with cross-links where paths intersect.

6. **Verify entry point optimization** for each audience segment. Ensure that the pages most likely to attract each segment are designed to serve as effective journey starting points.

7. **Audit the 70/20/10 ratio** across the full content network. Adjust content production plans to fill any ratio imbalances.

## Advanced Audience Journey Concepts

### Query Paths and Representative Queries
Map audience journeys based on representative queries, not just literal search terms. Search engines distribute relevance based on representative queries — the authoritative interpretation of search terms. "Board vision" and "vision board" trigger different representative queries with different journey paths. Identify the representative query for each journey stage.

### Historical Data Quality Per Journey Stage
Each journey touchpoint generates historical data — positive (quality engagement), negative (bounce, pogo-sticking), or neutral (brief impression). Non-quality sessions at ANY stage in the journey degrade historical data for ALL pages in that journey path. Design each touchpoint to maximize positive engagement signals: deep reading, internal link clicks, session completion.

### Click Path Optimization
Analyze click paths for conversion optimization. Keep page depth under 4 levels — deeper pages have negative usability and search engine evaluation impact. Short click paths for commercial content, longer exploratory paths for informational content. Use A/B testing to identify optimal paths to conversion.

### CTA Placement and Above-the-Fold Strategy
CTA should appear when the user is convinced of the product or service, NOT immediately. If placed too early, it signals aggressive commercialization before establishing value. Above-the-fold content establishes the page's identity and primary purpose. Design different landing page layouts for different traffic sources — informational visitors vs transactional visitors have different journey expectations.

### Session Satisfaction Mapping
Map complete search sessions, not individual queries. Search engines evaluate session satisfaction — did the user's full query sequence get resolved? Design journey touchpoints that anticipate the NEXT query in the session and provide answers proactively. This keeps users in your content network instead of returning to the SERP.

## Key Principles Summary

- Segment audiences into four levels: Novice (20-30%), Intermediate (40-50%), Advanced (15-25%), Professional (5-10%)
- Map five journey stages: Problem Awareness, Solution Awareness, Solution Evaluation, Decision, Retention
- Design 8-12 specific journey paths with internal linking connecting 3-5 pages per path
- Maintain a 70/20/10 content type ratio: informative, commercial, transactional
- Design distinct entry points for each audience segment
- The intermediate audience is the largest segment and deserves the most content investment
- Every page must link forward -- no dead ends in the journey
- Journey paths should cross-link where audience segments overlap or transition
