# Brand Identity & Content Voice Engine

This skill captures everything that makes a website feel like a *brand* rather than a content mill — the personality, the voice, the way you persuade, the promises you make, and the business outcomes you're driving toward. It then produces a Brand Identity Document that gets referenced by every other skill in the Semantic SEO workflow.

The core insight: Koray's framework optimizes for *search engine comprehension* (relevance, responsiveness, topical authority). This skill optimizes for *human connection* (trust, recognition, conversion). The two aren't in conflict — they're complementary layers. A page can follow every algorithmic authorship rule while still sounding unmistakably like your brand.

## When to Use This Skill

Invoke this skill as the **very first step** alongside `source-context` (Skill 1) when building any new website. The Brand Identity Document it produces becomes a persistent reference for:

- Content Brief Generation (Skill 8) — tone markers per section
- Algorithmic Authorship (Skill 12) — voice constraints within SEO rules
- Contextual Vector Optimizer (Skill 13) — CTA placement in the content flow
- Audience Journey Mapping (Skill 27) — messaging per funnel stage
- Content Brief Scaling (Skill 10) — consistent voice across 100+ articles
- Every piece of written content on the site

## Phase 1: Brand Discovery Interview

Before writing anything, gather the following from the user. Ask these questions conversationally — not as a checklist dump. Group them naturally across 2-3 exchanges.

### Business Foundation

1. **Mission Statement**: What is the core purpose of this business beyond making money? What change does it want to create in the world or in its customers' lives?
2. **Value Propositions**: What are the 3-5 specific promises you make to your audience? Not features — outcomes. "You'll rank higher" is a feature. "You'll stop losing traffic to competitors who publish worse content" is an outcome.
3. **Business Goals & KPIs**: What does success look like in 6 months? 12 months? Revenue targets, traffic goals, lead generation numbers, conversion rates, email list size, brand search volume — get specific.
4. **Monetization Model**: How exactly does money flow? Affiliate links, SaaS subscriptions, consulting, courses, ads, e-commerce? Each model shapes CTA strategy differently.
5. **Competitive Positioning**: Who are the 3-5 closest competitors? What do they do well? What do they do poorly? What gap does this brand fill that nobody else does?

### Brand Personality

6. **Brand Archetype**: If the brand were a person at a dinner party, how would they behave? Use one of the 12 Jungian archetypes as a starting framework, then customize:
   - The Sage (authority, expertise, clarity) — e.g., Koray's own brand
   - The Explorer (curiosity, adventure, independence) — e.g., Patagonia
   - The Creator (innovation, originality, vision) — e.g., Apple
   - The Caregiver (nurturing, protection, generosity) — e.g., Johnson & Johnson
   - The Hero (courage, achievement, mastery) — e.g., Nike
   - The Rebel (disruption, liberation, revolution) — e.g., Harley-Davidson
   - The Everyperson (belonging, honesty, relatability) — e.g., IKEA
   - The Jester (humor, fun, joy) — e.g., Old Spice
   - The Lover (passion, intimacy, connection) — e.g., Chanel
   - The Magician (transformation, vision, imagination) — e.g., Disney
   - The Ruler (control, stability, leadership) — e.g., Mercedes-Benz
   - The Innocent (simplicity, optimism, trust) — e.g., Dove

7. **3-Word Brand Personality**: Ask the user to describe the brand in exactly three adjectives. Examples: "Bold, technical, generous" or "Warm, no-nonsense, data-driven" or "Playful, contrarian, accessible."

8. **Anti-Personality**: What is the brand NOT? This is often more revealing than what it is. "We're never condescending," "We don't use corporate jargon," "We're not the cheapest option and we don't pretend to be."

### Voice & Tone

9. **Reading Level**: What's the target comprehension level? Hemingway-simple (Grade 6-8), professional but clear (Grade 9-11), or technical/academic (Grade 12+)?

10. **Sentence Style**: Short punchy sentences? Long flowing explanations? A mix that varies by section? Mirror the user's natural communication style from the conversation.

11. **Vocabulary Rules**:
    - **Power Words**: 10-15 words that should appear frequently across the site (e.g., "proven," "systematic," "transform," "clarity," "leverage")
    - **Forbidden Words**: Words the brand never uses (e.g., "cheap," "easy," "hack," "guru," "synergy," "utilize")
    - **Industry Terms**: Technical terms the audience already knows vs. terms that need explanation
    - **Branded Terms**: Proprietary phrases the brand owns (e.g., Koray's "Contextual Vector," "Cost of Retrieval Principle")

12. **Perspective & Pronouns**: First person ("I/We")? Second person ("You")? Third person ("One/The user")? Does it shift by content type?

13. **Formality Spectrum**: Place the brand on this scale:
    - 1 = Academic paper ("The data suggests a statistically significant correlation")
    - 3 = Professional blog ("Research shows a clear connection")
    - 5 = Conversational expert ("The data is pretty clear on this one")
    - 7 = Friendly peer ("So here's the deal — the numbers don't lie")
    - 9 = Casual social media ("Okay this data is wild, look at this")

14. **Humor Policy**: No humor? Occasional wit? Frequent humor? Self-deprecating? Dry? Sarcastic? Observational?

15. **Tone Variation by Content Type**: How does the voice shift across:
    - Pillar/cornerstone content (typically more authoritative)
    - How-to guides (typically more instructional and encouraging)
    - Product reviews/comparisons (typically more analytical and honest)
    - News/updates (typically more direct and timely)
    - About/team pages (typically more personal and warm)

### CTA Strategy

16. **Primary CTA per Funnel Stage**: What action do you want readers to take at each stage?
    - **Awareness** (just discovered you): Subscribe? Follow? Bookmark?
    - **Consideration** (comparing options): Download lead magnet? Free trial? Webinar?
    - **Decision** (ready to buy): Purchase? Book call? Sign up?
    - **Retention** (already a customer): Upsell? Referral? Community?

17. **CTA Voice**: How do CTAs sound? Aggressive ("Buy Now — Limited Time"), confident ("Start Your Free Trial"), warm ("Join 10,000+ marketers who..."), or subtle ("Learn more about how this works")?

18. **CTA Frequency**: How often should CTAs appear in content? Once at the end? After every major section? Inline within paragraphs? Sidebar persistent?

19. **Social Proof Style**: Testimonials with photos and names? Anonymous reviews? Case study numbers? Client logos? "As featured in" badges? Community size?

20. **Urgency & Scarcity Approach**: Does the brand use urgency ("Limited spots remaining")? Scarcity ("Only available this month")? Neither? Both? The answer should align with authenticity — forced urgency erodes trust for authority brands.

### Visual Language (for content formatting)

21. **Emoji Policy**: No emojis? Occasional? Frequent? Which ones align with brand?
22. **Formatting Personality**: Heavy use of bold and italic for emphasis? Clean minimal formatting? Callout boxes? Pull quotes?
23. **Image Style Direction**: Photography vs. illustration vs. data visualization? Warm vs. cool color palette? People vs. abstract? Professional vs. casual?

## 6. Integration Points with Semantic SEO Skills
This section maps exactly how brand identity feeds into each downstream skill:

### → Content Brief Generation (Skill 8)
- Add "Tone: [tone descriptor]" to every content brief header
- Include CTA placement instruction in the Contextual Structure column
- Add brand vocabulary to the required terms list

### → Algorithmic Authorship (Skill 12)
- Apply reading level constraint to all FS and PAA answers
- Use Power Words in bold answer phrases
- Enforce Forbidden Words filter on all output
- Match sentence style to brand formality level
- CTA sentences follow brand CTA voice, not generic patterns

### → Contextual Vector Optimizer (Skill 13)
- CTA sections map to specific positions in the contextual vector
- Social proof elements have designated heading levels
- The contextual flow must reflect brand storytelling arc (not just SEO logic)

### → Content Brief Scaling (Skill 10)
- Brand voice consistency markers in every scaled brief
- CTA templates scale with entity-specific variables
- Tone variation rules apply per content type across all briefs

### → Audience Journey Mapping (Skill 27)
- Each journey stage gets its own tone calibration from Section 3
- CTA progression follows the funnel map from Section 4
- Messaging per segment uses personality-appropriate language

### → Micro-Semantics (Skill 14)
- Answer formatting respects brand style (e.g., no bullet lists if brand prefers prose)
- Passage voice must match section-level tone from the brief

### → SERP Analysis (Skill 28)
- Featured snippet answers use brand voice within the 40-word limit
- PAA answers maintain brand personality in single-sentence definitive answers

### → Root Document Brief (Skill 9)

- The root sets the brand voice standard for the entire network

## Phase 3: Voice Calibration Test

After generating the Brand Identity Document, validate it with a quick calibration test. Write the same paragraph in 3 variations:

1. **Generic SEO voice** (what a content mill would produce)
2. **Brand voice applied** (using the Brand Identity Document)
3. **Over-applied brand voice** (exaggerated to show the boundary)

Show all three to the user and ask: "Does #2 sound like you? Is there anything from #3 that you actually want to keep, or does it go too far?"

This calibration catches misunderstandings early — before the voice propagates across 200+ articles.

## Advanced Brand Identity Concepts

### Brand SERP Strategy
Optimize your Brand SERP (the search results page for your exact brand name). Target these SERP features: Knowledge Panel, People Also Ask about your brand, Videos, Images, Reviews, Top Stories, Similar Entities, and Social Media profiles. Each feature reinforces trust and reputation signals. Monitor competitors' Brand SERPs to identify trust signal gaps.

### Entity Identity in Knowledge Graph
Your brand must become a recognized entity in search engines' knowledge graphs. This requires: consistent information across authoritative sources (Wikipedia, Wikidata, social profiles, industry directories), proper schema markup (Organization, LocalBusiness), and sufficient document statistics (mentions from diverse, authoritative sources). Entity identity shapes how search engines present your brand across ALL search results, not just branded queries.

### Source Context as Brand Signal
Source context must be reflected on every page of your website — in boilerplate, main content, and supplementary content. The site-wide N-grams created from your brand identity and central entity must appear consistently. This is how search engines understand that your ENTIRE website serves a unified purpose and audience. Inconsistent source context signals across pages weaken the brand's entity identity.

### Brand Search Demand Monitoring
Compare your brand's search demand with competitors. Brand search demand is a direct signal of entity prominence. Track: branded query volume, branded query variations, brand + attribute query combinations. Growing brand search demand indicates increasing entity prominence; declining demand signals identity erosion.

## How This Skill Connects to the Workflow

The Brand Identity Document produced by this skill becomes a **persistent input** to the Semantic SEO pipeline. Think of it this way:

- **Source Context** (Skill 1) tells every skill *what* to write about
- **Brand Identity** (Skill 31) tells every skill *how* to write it

Both are created at Stage 1. Both are referenced by every downstream skill. Neither changes frequently — but when they do change, the effects cascade through the entire content network, just like changing an anchor text cascades through the semantic content network.

The principle is the same as Koray's framework: create one authoritative source of truth, then let it flow systematically through every output. Inconsistent brand voice across a 300-article site is the branding equivalent of conflicting facts across pages — it erodes trust, confuses both humans and machines, and makes the whole network weaker than the sum of its parts.
